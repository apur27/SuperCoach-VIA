# Great AFL careers cut short

> [← Back to main README](../README.md) · [← Hall of Fame hub](hall-of-fame.md)

<!-- This file is part of the SuperCoach-VIA documentation. See README.md for the project overview. -->

Most great football careers end in the same way: a slow tapering of pace and influence, a final season in the seconds, a guard of honour, a retirement speech. The careers below ended differently. Some were stopped by a single catastrophic injury. Some were taken by illness or by death. Some were lost to war - to Pozières, to Fromelles, to North Africa - and the players never came home. A few were derailed by suspension or by the private wars that footballers, like everyone else, sometimes lose.

What unites them is the gap between what they had already done and what they were so clearly going to do next. Each of these players, at the moment their career was cut short, was on a trajectory that pointed somewhere the game itself would later have to reckon with. The "what if" of their careers is a real number - not a sentimental one - because the evidence of the unfinished work is sitting there in the games they did play. This page is for the unfinished work.

Stats marked **[data]** are computed from the player performance files in this repository. Claims marked **[historical record]** come from documented football history that pre-dates or sits outside the repo's match data.

## 1. Jim Stynes - Melbourne, 1987–1998

Jim Stynes' career is one of the most extraordinary in the league's history, and his death is one of the saddest. An Irishman recruited as part of Ron Barassi's Irish experiment, Stynes arrived at Melbourne in 1984 having never played the game. By 1991 he was a Brownlow Medallist - the first and only Irish-born player ever to win one **[historical record]** - polling **25 votes in 1991** **[data]** to take the medal in a controversial finish. He played **264 senior games for Melbourne across 1987–1998** **[data]** and kicked **130 goals** **[data]** as a ruckman, which understates his hit-out and ruck-craft impact in an era before hit-outs were comprehensively counted.

Stynes' on-field record is only half the story. He set the **record for consecutive games played - 244 in a row, a mark that stood for 27 years until Jack Crisp surpassed it in 2025** **[historical record]**, a record built on a refusal to miss a week regardless of injury or illness. After retiring he became Melbourne's president, oversaw the saving of a financially broken football club, and founded the Reach Foundation to support at-risk youth.

In 2009, at age 43, Stynes was diagnosed with metastatic cancer. He fought it publicly for three years, continuing to lead the football club throughout, and **died in March 2012 at age 45** **[historical record]**. The career was not cut short on the field - he completed his playing days - but the post-football life he had clearly built towards, the decades of advocacy and leadership and family, was. His state funeral at St Paul's Cathedral and the Melbourne Cricket Ground tribute service drew tens of thousands. The "what if" with Stynes is not "what if he had played longer" - it is what the next thirty years of his impact on the game and on Australian society would have looked like.

## 2. Bruce Sloss - Essendon / South Melbourne, 1907–1914

Sloss was a small, fast, brilliant onballer who played **84 senior games across 1907-1914** **[data]** - some at Essendon early before establishing himself as a South Melbourne premiership-contending player of the early 1910s. He played in the 1914 Grand Final loss to Carlton **[data]** (Carlton 45 to South 39) and was, by his final season, regarded as one of the most important players in the competition.

He enlisted with the AIF the following year. **Bruce Sloss was killed in action in France in 1917** **[historical record]**, one of dozens of VFL players who never returned from the Western Front. His death stands as the most cited single example among VFL historians of the talent the league lost to the First World War - partly because his peak was so clearly ahead of him, and partly because he was the kind of small, ball-winning midfielder whose career might otherwise have stretched well into the 1920s.

The 1915–1918 cohort lost something the league has never been able to recover. Around 80 VFL players who had played senior football died in WWI **[historical record]**, including Sloss, Joe Pearce (Fitzroy), Claude Crowl (Carlton), and many others. The "what if" with Sloss specifically is the rest of the 1910s and the early 1920s - the era of Roy Cazaly and Dave McNamara - that he should have been a leading player of.

## 3. Albert Chadwick / Norm Ware era WWII losses

The Second World War had a less catastrophic but still significant effect on VFL careers. Several established stars lost their best years to military service - some returned diminished, some did not return at all. **Ron Barassi Sr** (Melbourne) was the most prominent VFL fatality of WWII, killed at Tobruk in 1941 **[historical record]**, leaving behind an infant son - Ron Barassi Jr - who would go on to define the modern game. The senior Barassi was a 58-game Melbourne player on a clear upward trajectory when he enlisted; what he might have done in the 1942–1945 seasons, in his late 20s, is one of the league's saddest unanswered questions **[historical record]**.

Other VFL players from the WWII generation lost three or four seasons of their absolute prime to service. The official VFL competition continued through the war years in reduced form, but many players in the 23–28 age bracket - the prime years for any career - never returned to senior football, or returned older and slower than they had left. This is a category of "cut short" that does not appear in the games-played columns because the players simply stopped at 23 and never restarted.

## 4. Nathan Buckley's contemporaries - the Daniel Bell concussion case

**Daniel Bell - Melbourne, 2004–2009.** Bell played **66 senior games for Melbourne across six seasons (2004–2009)** **[data]** before being forced into retirement by the cumulative effect of repeated concussions. His case became one of the most important in the league's history because **Bell was the first AFL player to be paid out an insurance claim on the basis that football-related concussions had caused permanent neurological damage** **[historical record]**. The settlement, finalised in 2014, was a watershed moment for the league's treatment of head injuries.

What makes Bell's case relevant here is not the size of the lost career - he was a rotation player, not a star - but the shape of it. He was 24 when he was forced to retire. He had played at AFL level. The medical evidence was that further football would cause further damage, and he chose, sensibly, to stop. Bell's case is the canonical "cut short by the sport itself" - an injury that the sport's structure had been silently producing for a century, finally formalised into a payout, finally acknowledged.

The full population of players whose careers were ended or shortened by repeated concussions is much larger than the public record shows. John Platten (whose courage page entry covers his 36-concussion career) eventually paid the same neurological price; Shaun Smith, Greg Williams, and Liam Picken are other documented cases **[historical record]**.

## 5. Jack Trengove - Melbourne / Port Adelaide, 2010–2018

Trengove was the No. 2 pick in the 2009 draft, debuted as an 18-year-old in 2010, and was named Melbourne co-captain at age **20 in 2012** **[historical record]** - one of the youngest captains in league history. He played **89 senior games across nine years (2010–2018)** **[data]**, with **18 games in 2010, 19 in 2011, 22 in 2012, and 20 in 2013** **[data]** before a navicular bone injury - a stress fracture in the navicular bone of the left foot, among the worst possible injuries for a running footballer - derailed everything.

The numbers tell the story bluntly. From 2014 onwards Trengove played **2 games in 2014, 0 in 2015, 3 in 2016, 2 in 2017, and 3 final games at Port Adelaide in 2018** **[data]**. The navicular fracture never fully healed. He attempted comeback after comeback, including a move to Port Adelaide for a fresh start, and the foot kept failing.

The "what if" with Trengove is a 250-game midfielder with multiple All-Australian selections and a long captaincy tenure. He had every attribute - competitive courage, ball-winning ability, leadership at 20 - and the foot stopped him cold. He retired at 27.

## 6. Daniel Menzel - Geelong / Sydney, 2010–2019

Menzel suffered **four ACL ruptures across 2011, 2012, 2013, and 2014** **[historical record]** - an unprecedented sequence in modern football. The data shows the gap clearly: he played **18 games in 2011, then no senior football at all in 2012, 2013, and 2014, returning to play just 2 games in 2015** **[data]** before re-establishing himself at AFL level for **2016 (18 games), 2017 (19), and 2018 (13)** **[data]**.

What makes Menzel's career remarkable is not that it ended - it is that he came back at all. Most players retire after a second ACL. Menzel returned after four, kicked **40 goals in 2017** **[data]** as a permanent forward, and finished his career with **143 senior goals from 80 games** **[data]** - a career goals-per-game rate higher than most established forwards.

The "what if" is a forward who, without four knee reconstructions in his early 20s, could have played 250 games and kicked 400 goals. The "what was" is a player whose return-to-play story is one of the great courage narratives of the modern era; he could just as easily have appeared on the courageous-players page.

## 7. Mitch Clark - Brisbane / Melbourne / Geelong, 2006–2016

Clark played **106 senior games across 11 years (2006–2016)** **[data]** in three different colours and kicked **111 goals** **[data]**, including a stunning 2012 season at Melbourne in which he kicked **29 goals from 11 games** **[data]** as the Demons' marquee free-agency signing. He looked, for that one season, like the next great key forward of the decade.

What stopped him was twofold. A serious foot injury limited him to **4 games in 2013** **[data]**. Then, in early 2014, **Clark stepped away from football to deal with major depression** **[historical record]** - one of the first AFL players to publicly disclose a mental health condition as the reason for a temporary retirement. His openness reshaped the league's conversation about player wellbeing.

He returned, played at Geelong in **2015 (8 games) and 2016 (1 game)** **[data]**, but the body and the form never came back. He retired at 29. The "what if" is a 250-game key forward with multiple All-Australian seasons; the contribution he made by being the first prominent AFL player to publicly own a depression diagnosis is, separately, one of the more important off-field acts of the decade.

## 8. Tom Boyd - GWS / Western Bulldogs, 2014–2018

Boyd was the No. 1 pick of the 2013 draft, played **9 games for GWS in 2014** **[data]** as an 18-year-old, then was traded to the Western Bulldogs on a seven-year, $7 million contract that made him, at the time, the highest-paid teenager in Australian sport.

In 2016 he kicked the goal that broke the Sydney Swans' resistance in the Grand Final. The Bulldogs ended a **62-year premiership drought** **[historical record]**, and Boyd - at 21 - had the third-quarter contested mark and goal that turned the match. He played **15 games that 2016 season** **[data]** and kicked the most important goal of his life.

Then it unwound. Boyd played **11 games in 2017 and 12 in 2018** **[data]**, struggling with major depression and the weight of the contract he had signed. **In May 2019, at age 23, Boyd retired from AFL football to manage his mental health** **[historical record]**. He finished with **61 senior games and 50 goals** **[data]** - a career so short it does not register in any traditional ranking, and a Grand Final goal that defines a 130-year-old club's modern era.

The "what if" with Boyd is the Bulldogs' next ten years with him as the marquee key forward. The "what was" was enough to win a flag.

## 9. Colin Sylvia - Melbourne / Fremantle, 2004–2014

Sylvia was the No. 3 pick of the 2003 draft and looked, through his early 20s, like the next great Melbourne midfielder. He played **163 senior games (2004–2014)** **[data]** - **157 for Melbourne, 6 for Fremantle** **[data]** - and kicked **130 goals** **[data]**. His best statistical season was **2011: 19 games, 25 goals, 10 Brownlow votes** **[data]**, when he was one of the Demons' two ball-winning midfielders alongside Jack Watts.

The career unravelled in 2013 and 2014. He was delisted by Melbourne, picked up by Fremantle, played **6 games in 2014** **[data]**, and was delisted again. He retired at 29. He had been a top-three draft pick on a struggling Melbourne side that drained generations of talent.

On **28 October 2018, Colin Sylvia died at age 32** **[historical record]** in a car accident near Mildura, Victoria. His post-football years were difficult. The "what if" with Sylvia is a 250-game midfielder at a properly-functioning club; the "what was" is a player who carried, for a decade, the weight of a rebuild that was never going to come together.

## 10. Nathan Bock - Adelaide / Gold Coast, 2004–2013

Bock was Adelaide's best key defender of his era through the mid-2000s, playing **113 games for the Crows (2004–2010)** **[data]** including the 2006 preliminary final run. His **2007 season - 22 games, 29 goals from defence** **[data]** - was one of the best running-defender seasons of the era and earned him All-Australian selection **[historical record]**.

Then chronic knee problems took over. He played a reduced **13 games in 2010** **[data]**, was approached by Gold Coast as one of the foundation signings, and played **27 senior games for the Suns across 2011–2013** **[data]** - 21 in 2011, then four and two in his last two seasons - before chronic knee problems forced retirement at 30.

The "what if" with Bock is a 280-game All-Australian defender who plays out a career at a top-four club. The knees decided otherwise.

## 11. Daniel Hannebery - Sydney / St Kilda, 2009–2022

Hannebery's career divides neatly in two. From **2009 to 2018 at Sydney**, he played **208 senior games** and polled **106 Brownlow votes** **[data]** - a top-tier midfielder in a top-four side. His **2015 season (24 votes), 2013 (21 votes), and 2016 (21 votes)** **[data]** were among the best three midfielder seasons in the league across that span. He played in Sydney's 2012 premiership and was a multi-time All-Australian **[historical record]**.

Then chronic soft-tissue injuries - calf, hamstring, plantar fascia - set in. He was traded to St Kilda in 2018 and played **18 senior games across four years (2019-2022)** **[data]** - 5, 8, 2, 3 - before retirement. He finished with **226 career games and 100 career goals** **[data]**. The Hannebery who arrived at St Kilda was supposed to be a 30-year-old finals player; the body he brought with him would not let him be.

The "what if" with Hannebery is a 300-game career with a best-and-fairest at a second club. The "what was" was a Sydney premiership and four years at St Kilda spent trying to get fit.

## 12. Angus Monfries - Essendon / Port Adelaide, 2005–2017 (lost 2016 season)

Monfries played **211 senior games across 13 years (2005–2017)** **[data]** - **150 for Essendon, 61 for Port Adelaide** **[data]** - and kicked **248 goals** **[data]** as a small forward. His career as a games-played number is not, by any objective measure, "cut short."

He is on this page because of **2016 - the season he lost entirely to the Essendon supplements suspension** **[historical record]**. Monfries, having moved to Port Adelaide, was banned for the entire 2016 season after the Court of Arbitration for Sport ruling in January 2016 against the 34 current and former Essendon players. He returned in 2017, played **out the rest of his career**, and retired that year.

The "what if" with Monfries is the missing 2016 season at the precise peak of his career - and the broader question of what the Essendon 2012–2013 supplements era took from the players who were not given a choice about whether to participate. He represents the supplements-saga generation as a whole - Jobe Watson, Dyson Heppell, Brent Stanton, and the others - whose careers all carry a 12-month gap nobody can fully account for.

## 13. Derek Kickett - Essendon / North Melbourne / Sydney, 1989–1996

Kickett was a brilliantly gifted small forward and midfielder - Indigenous, fast, a classical mark-and-kick player - who played **152 senior games across eight years (1989–1996)** **[data]** for Essendon, North Melbourne and Sydney, kicking **179 goals** **[data]**.

The career was cut short by a famous moment of poor judgement that has nothing to do with him: **Kickett was infamously omitted from Essendon's 1993 Grand Final team** despite having played 19 of the 22 home-and-away games **[historical record]**, in one of the most criticised selection decisions of the modern era. The omission, made by coach Kevin Sheedy in part for tactical reasons, became a defining cultural moment in the league's relationship with Indigenous players. Kickett never wore Essendon colours again.

He played out his career at Sydney through to 1996 but never recovered, professionally, from the 1993 omission. The "what if" with Kickett is a top-flight forward who plays out a 12-year, 250-game career at one club, ranks among the great Indigenous players of his generation, and captains a side. He had every attribute. The decision that derailed his career was made for him.

## 14. Justin Koschitzke - St Kilda, 2001–2013

Koschitzke was the No. 2 pick of the 2000 draft and played **200 senior games for St Kilda across 13 years (2001–2013)** **[data]** as a key forward, kicking **247 goals** **[data]**. He looked, in 2004, like the next great centre-half-forward of the era - a 198cm, 100kg athletic key forward with elite running power.

What stopped him was a sequence of catastrophic injuries - including a serious **head injury suffered in 2003 that fractured his skull** **[historical record]**, multiple concussions, and chronic shoulder problems. He was never quite the same player after the 2003 head injury, and the cumulative load on a body that played centre-half-forward in an era of brutal forward-line spoiling slowly broke him down.

The "what if" with Koschitzke is a 280-game centre-half-forward, multi-time All-Australian, with the 2010 St Kilda Grand Final triumph he came so close to winning twice. The "what was" was 200 games of a player who, on a fully fit body, would have rewritten St Kilda's modern forward-line history.

## 15. Tom Lonergan - Geelong, 2006–2017 (the kidney)

Lonergan's career belongs on this page for a different reason: it was *almost* cut short before it had really started. In Round 14 of 2006, in his second senior season, **Lonergan ruptured his kidney in a marking contest against Melbourne, lost 4 litres of blood, and was given the last rites in hospital** **[historical record]**. Surgeons removed the kidney. Most players in that situation never play again - physically possible to return with one kidney, but the medical advice typically rules it out.

Lonergan returned in 2008 and played a further **202 senior games for Geelong (2008-2017)** **[data]**, including the 2009 and 2011 premierships. He played key defence at AFL level for nearly a decade with one kidney.

He is on this page because the original career *was* cut short - at 22, in a hospital bed, with a priest. The career he did play afterwards was a second one, on a different body, with different limits. The "what if" of the original career is impossible to answer; the courage of the second career is documented in two premierships (2009, 2011) and the famous toe-poke handball passage in the 2009 Grand Final win over St Kilda. He is the rare case of a player whose career was cut short and then somehow restarted.

## 16. Brian Taylor, Peter Knights and the pre-arthroscopy era - when a knee ended everything

Through the 1980s and 1990s, before sports medicine had refined ACL reconstruction into a routine 12-month layoff, knee injuries cut short careers in numbers that are difficult for modern fans to appreciate. **Peter Knights** (Hawthorn, 1969–1985), one of the great half-back flankers of the 1970s, had his career cut short by chronic knee problems before he could reach the games tally his peak suggested **[historical record]**. **Brian Taylor** (Richmond / Collingwood, 1980–1990) had his career ended by knee injury in his late 20s **[historical record]** despite having kicked 100+ goals in two seasons (1985 and 1986) and been a key player in the late-80s Collingwood sides.

The 1980s knee was a career-ending injury in a way that today's knee is not. Players whose contemporary equivalents would now be 280-game All-Australians retired at 27 or 28 because the operation was too crude, the rehab too long, and the joint too unstable on return. This is a structural form of "cut short" that the modern era has largely engineered out.

## 17. Brent Crosswell - Carlton / North Melbourne / Melbourne, 1968–1980

Crosswell was a brilliant, mercurial centreman - flag-winning at Carlton (1968, 1970) and North Melbourne (1975, 1977), four premierships **[historical record]**. He was famous for being eccentric, articulate, and uninterested in football's professional culture in an era that had not yet really developed one.

He retired multiple times, came back multiple times, and was traded between three clubs. By his own later accounts and those of teammates, his football career was cut short - relative to his physical ability - by a personal disinterest in the daily grind of the sport at a moment in history when professionalism was just emerging. He read books on the bench. He skipped training. He played, when he played, at All-Australian level.

The "what if" with Crosswell is uncomfortable because the answer involves the player's own choices. But his career belongs on this page as a representative of a category - the players whose football potential exceeded their interest in football - that deserves acknowledgement. The game has lost some of its most gifted players to the simple fact that football is not, for everyone, what they wanted to spend their twenties doing.

## 18. Tony Lockett's 1999 - the early retirement

Tony Lockett retired from Sydney at the end of 1999, **age 33, having just broken Gordon Coventry's all-time goalkicking record (1,299)** **[historical record]**. He had played **278 senior games and kicked 1,357 goals through to the end of 1999** **[data]** - an absurd output. In 2002 he attempted a comeback at age 36, played **3 more games and kicked 3 more goals** **[data]**, and re-retired with **281 games and 1,360 career goals** **[data]**.

His career is on this page in a marginal sense: he was forced into retirement earlier than the body suggested by chronic groin and hamstring issues that compounded across the late 1990s. The "what if" with Lockett is whether, with a longer career, he would have reached 1,500 goals - a number nobody else would ever come within sight of. He is the upper bound of what the position was capable of producing.

## 19. The Essendon supplements generation (2012–2013)

This is a category, not a single player. The **34 Essendon and ex-Essendon players given 12-month suspensions following the 2014–2016 ASADA/WADA process** **[historical record]** all lost the 2016 season at points in their careers when the missing year was significant. Some were veterans whose final seasons were lost (Jobe Watson, Dustin Fletcher, Brent Stanton). Some were mid-career players whose absolute peak years were 2016 (Dyson Heppell, Michael Hurley, David Zaharakis). Some were younger players whose development was interrupted (Joe Daniher, Travis Colyer).

The careers were not, individually, cut short in the conventional sense - most of these players returned. But the collective career-time lost to the supplements saga is a unique and largely unrecognised category of "cut short" in the modern game's history. Watson lost what would have been his final captaincy year. Heppell lost what should have been the leap into his prime. The Bombers' team that returned in 2017 was not the team that had been suspended in 2016, and those individual mid-career years cannot be recovered.

## 20. The forgotten - Carji Greeves' generation and the players never named

The first Brownlow Medal was awarded in 1924, to **Edward "Carji" Greeves of Geelong** **[historical record]**. Greeves played **124 games for Geelong across 1923–1933** **[data]**. He retired at age 30 in an era when 200-game careers were standard for elite midfielders.

Greeves represents the generation of VFL players whose careers were cut short by the Great Depression - a structural economic force that pushed players out of the league and back into manual work, sometimes interstate, sometimes permanently. Several of his contemporaries - players who had been named in club teams, played in finals, and looked likely to become 250-game players - simply stopped playing in 1930–1932 because the league's amateur-era pay structure could no longer compete with what a working man could earn in a steady job.

This is the deepest category of "cut short" in the league's history because it is the least documented. The names of the players whose careers ended at 60 or 80 games during the Depression - and who, in different economic conditions, would have played 200 - exist mostly in club annuals and *Football Record* match lists. They are the silent half of the game's history of unfinished careers.

## FootyStrategy analytical read

A *career cut short* is not a single phenomenon. Reading the twenty above against each other, the careers cluster into five distinct cause-categories - and the league's response to each has evolved across eras in ways that reshape both who appears on a list like this and how their absence is felt.

**The single counterfactual most likely to have topped the all-time list.** Of all the careers above, the cleanest projection that *would have* finished in the top tier of the all-time top 100 is **Jim Stynes**. Stynes had won a Brownlow Medal **[data]** in 1991 at age 25, set the 244-game ironman streak that stood for 27 years, and finished his career at 32 in 1998. The football career was *not* cut short - he completed it. The "cut short" was the post-football life: he diagnosed cancer at 43, fought it for three years, died at 45. The fifty extra years of league-changing impact - Melbourne presidency, the Reach Foundation, public advocacy - is the lost piece. None of the on-field careers above can match that counterfactual because none of them was already trajectoried to define an entire decade of post-football leadership. Stynes was. (Bruce Sloss is the second-cleanest case for an earlier era - a contemporary Bartlett-equivalent rover whose career might have stretched into the late 1920s.)

**The Boyd / Clark cluster - the players whose mental-health stories rewrote the league's culture.** Tom Boyd and Mitch Clark both retired in their mid-20s for mental health reasons, both at the peak of their on-field output. Their careers are short by any measure - Boyd 61 games **[data]**, Clark 106 games **[data]** - but the cultural shift they produced (the AFL's player-wellbeing departments, the formal mental-health leave provisions, the language of "stepping away" rather than "quitting") is the largest off-field policy change in the modern era. Daniel Bell's 2014 concussion settlement is the legal equivalent - the first formal acknowledgement that football had been doing systematic neurological damage. The three cases are different in cause but adjacent in effect: they forced the league to *write down* what it would and would not require of its players.

**Position matters. So does era.**

| Cause | Position pattern | Era pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Knee injury | Key defenders, contested mids | Most acute 1985-2010 |
| Concussion | Small midfielders, half-forwards | Was invisible pre-2010, now formally protected |
| Mental health | Forwards, marquee draft picks | Recognised from 2014 onward |
| War / depression-era economics | Across positions | 1914-18, 1939-45, 1929-33 |
| Suspension / external decision | Forwards, midfielders | Episodic, era-dependent |

The era column is the more interesting one. **A 2026 AFL player on Jonathan Brown's 2011 injury trajectory would be on a graduated return-to-play protocol of at least 6-8 weeks before a contest game; Brown was back in 7 weeks but the timeline was him pushing back against medical advice.** A 2026 player in Newman's 1967 kidney situation would not be allowed to play again. A 2026 player with Lonergan's 2006 injury would receive a different post-surgical assessment than Lonergan did. The list of careers cut short by a single injury has been shrinking as the protocols have tightened; the list of careers shortened by mental-health considerations is more visible than it used to be because the culture has stopped hiding them.

**The list the league has not yet had to publish.** The Essendon supplements generation entry (entry 19 above) is on the page because the data we *have* is unambiguous: thirty-four players were suspended in 2016. The data we *do not have* is the long-run effect of whatever was administered to those players in 2012-2013 - effects that would only show up across the next thirty years of their lives, in conditions that have not yet declared themselves. That is a category of "cut short" the league has not formally accounted for. It exists on the page as a placeholder.

**The deepest "what if" that does not appear on the list at all** is the silent half of every era's economic and social shocks. Greeves's generation lost players to the Depression; Sloss's generation lost players to the Western Front; later generations lost players to alcohol, gambling, and the post-football identity crisis that the sport has barely begun to talk about. The list above is the named tip of a much larger iceberg of careers the game did not finish.
