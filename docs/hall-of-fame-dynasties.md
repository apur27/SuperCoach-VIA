# Great AFL dynasties

> [← Back to main README](../README.md) · [← Hall of Fame hub](hall-of-fame.md)

<!-- This file is part of the SuperCoach-VIA documentation. See README.md for the project overview. -->

A dynasty in Australian rules football is rarely just a run of premierships. It is the moment when a club's culture, methods, players, or coaches stop being a single team and start being a force that reshapes the rest of the competition. Some of the entries below are pure premiership runs - Collingwood's Machine, Hawthorn's two great waves, Brisbane's three-peat. Others are dynasties of influence: a coach whose disciples spread out and built three more flags between them, or a club whose departing players went on to anchor someone else's premiership.

What unites them is permanence. The 1989 Hawthorn Grand Final is over; the way Hawthorn taught the league to play running football is not. The 2017–2020 Richmond defensive press is no longer being executed by Tigers; it is being executed, in modified form, by half the league. A dynasty is the part of football history that keeps showing up in the next era's tape.

Stats marked **[data]** are computed from the player performance files and match records in this repository. Claims marked **[historical record]** come from documented football history that pre-dates or sits outside the repo's match data.

## 1. Collingwood "The Machine" - 1927–1930

Four consecutive VFL premierships **[historical record]** - a feat no other club has ever matched, in any era, in any state. The Magpies won the Grand Finals of 1927 (Collingwood def. Richmond), 1928 (def. Richmond), 1929 (def. Richmond), and 1930 (def. Geelong) **[data]**, the only four-peat in the league's history. The 1929 side won every game they played that season, finishing the home-and-away rounds undefeated and then taking the flag - a record of total in-season dominance that has not been threatened since.

The Machine was built around **Gordon Coventry**, the era-defining full-forward whose **1,299 career goals from 306 games** **[data]** stood as the all-time record for sixty years until Tony Lockett broke it in 1999. Coventry kicked **17 in a single game** **[data]** at his peak, and his 1929 season alone produced enough goals to win modern Coleman Medals comfortably. Around him the Magpies fielded **Syd Coventry** (his brother and four-time premiership captain), **Albert Collier**, and **Harry Collier** - a list so deep that the side could lose multiple stars to representative duties and not drop a game.

Collingwood's dominance reshaped the league's economics. Crowds at Victoria Park became the biggest in the competition. The 1928 Grand Final drew **50,026** **[data]** in the depths of the late-1920s downturn, and the 1929 final drew **63,336** **[data]** - numbers that rival a 2020s Grand Final pro rata. The "Machine" name was not marketing; opponents used it because the side functioned with an almost industrial regularity that nobody had previously seen in a league still played by amateurs and tradesmen.

The 1929 squad's premiership came at the start of the Great Depression, and the dynasty's tail in 1930 effectively closed the league's pre-Depression era. Several of the Machine's stars retired into financial hardship over the following years; the structural impact of the 1930s on careers (covered in the [careers cut short](hall-of-fame-careers-cut-short.md) page) particularly affected this generation. But the four flags stand alone, and they will continue to stand alone - no twenty-first-century club has ever come within reach of consecutive years three and four.

## 2. Melbourne under Norm Smith - 1955–1964

Six premierships in ten years **[historical record]**: 1955, 1956, 1957 (a three-peat), 1959, 1960, and 1964. Verified from the match data in this repo: Melbourne def. Collingwood in the 1955, 1956, 1960 and 1964 Grand Finals; Melbourne def. Essendon in the 1957 and 1959 Grand Finals **[data]**. Six flags in a decade is a strike rate that has never been matched in the modern era.

The Demons' dynasty was the work of **Norm Smith** as coach and **Ron Barassi** as captain and on-ground organiser. Barassi played **204 senior games for Melbourne (1953–1964)** **[data]** - the entire arc of the dynasty - kicking **256 goals** **[data]** as a centre half-forward and ruck-rover. He was the first player in the league to be specifically deployed as a "ruck-rover," a position Smith had effectively invented to maximise Barassi's contested-ball strength and ruck mobility. The position has been a fixture of every premiership team for the seventy years since.

The Smith-Barassi Demons defined modern coaching in two ways. First, they built around a positional innovation rather than around a single dominant player - the ruck-rover role was structural, not personal, and could be filled by different players over time. Second, they professionalised the Melbourne match preparation in a way no other club had: Smith's training sessions were systematic, drill-based, and tactical, where most contemporaries still ran straight-line laps. The dynasty was as much a methodology as it was a list of flags.

When the dynasty ended in 1964 it ended hard: Barassi famously left for Carlton in 1965 to take up a captain-coach role, and Melbourne would not win another flag for **57 years**, until 2021 **[historical record]**. Smith was sacked, briefly, in 1965 before being reinstated, and the chemistry that had produced six flags in ten years dissipated within two seasons. The lesson for every subsequent dynasty was that the ingredients are perishable; even the best coach and the best on-ground leader cannot sustain a dynasty without the third element of a stable list, and Melbourne's was about to be cannibalised by the broader competition's belated effort to catch up.

## 3. Carlton's long era - 1968–1982

Five premierships in fifteen seasons (1968, 1970, 1972, 1979, 1981, 1982 - and a sixth Grand Final loss in 1973) **[data]**. The Carlton era was unusual among dynasties because it bridged two distinct generations of player and two coaching regimes: the late-1960s Ron Barassi-coached Carlton that took the 1968 and 1970 flags, and the late-1970s/early-1980s David Parkin / Robert Walls Carlton that took 1979, 1981, and 1982. The 1972 premiership sat between them and was the high-water mark of Carlton's running game.

The personnel anchor across both halves was **Alex Jesaulenko** ("Jezza"), who played **256 games for Carlton (1967–1979)** and **23 for St Kilda (1980–1981)**, kicking **444 career goals** **[data]**. His mark in the 1970 Grand Final - the famous "*Jesaulenko, you beauty*" mark on Graeme Jenkin's shoulders - is the single most-replayed image in pre-television-era VFL history **[historical record]**. The 1970 flag itself, in which Carlton came back from 44 points down at half-time against Collingwood to win, is the canonical comeback Grand Final and the structural foundation of the modern half-time-deficit-is-recoverable mythology.

The second-wave Carlton (1979–1982) was built around **Justin Madden**, who played **287 senior games for the Blues (1983–1996)** **[data]** as the dominant ruckman of the early 1980s, **Bruce Doull** at half-back, and **Mike Fitzpatrick** as captain-ruckman. Carlton's structural innovation in this period was the use of the ruck as a secondary midfielder - Madden was expected to win hit-outs, run the ground, and contribute disposals - which prefigured the modern mobile-ruck role by twenty years.

Carlton's dynasty also had a coaching tree of its own. **David Parkin** coached the 1981 and 1982 flags, then went on to a long career across multiple clubs; **Robert Walls** coached the 1987 Carlton flag (a tail-end addition to the era), and the methods that produced Carlton's running game in 1972 were absorbed by the league at large. By the mid-1980s every premiership side ran more handballs per game than any 1970s side, and the Carlton template was the reason.

## 4. Hawthorn 1971–1991 - the greatest dynasty in VFL/AFL history

Eight premierships in twenty-one years (1971, 1976, 1978, 1983, 1986, 1988, 1989, 1991) - verified Grand Final results in this repo confirm Hawthorn def. St Kilda 1971 (82-75), def. North Melbourne 1976 (100-70), def. North Melbourne 1978 (121-103), def. Essendon 1983 (140-57), def. Carlton 1986 (110-68), def. Melbourne 1988 (152-56), def. Geelong 1989 (144-138), def. West Coast 1991 (139-86) **[data]**. All eight premierships in the window are verifiable from the match-record CSVs in this repo. This is the greatest concentrated premiership era any single VFL/AFL club has produced.

The dynasty was the work of two waves. The first, coached by **John Kennedy** (whose famous "*do something*" half-time speech is still quoted), produced the 1971, 1976 and 1978 sides. The second, coached by **Allan Jeans** and then **Alan Joyce**, produced the 1983, 1986, 1988, 1989, and 1991 sides. The bridge between the two - the player who connected both waves - was **Michael Tuck**, who played the all-time-record **426 games for Hawthorn (1972–1991)** **[data]** and won **seven premierships** as a player **[historical record]**: more than any modern-era player. Tuck's final flag in 1991 came nineteen years after his debut.

The on-field stars across the era are a who's-who of all-time greats. **Leigh Matthews** played **332 games and kicked 915 goals for Hawthorn (1969–1985)** **[data]** - the highest goalkicker among the all-time top five players (he sits at No. 2 on this project's all-time top 100 ranking) **[data]**. **Jason Dunstall** kicked **1,254 goals from 269 games for Hawthorn (1985–1998)** **[data]**, a goal-per-game rate that no other modern key forward has approached, and he played in all four of the 1986–1991 flags. **John Platten** played **258 games and kicked 228 goals (1986–1997)** **[data]** as the ball-winning small midfielder of the second wave. **Dermott Brereton** played **189 games for Hawthorn (1982–1992)** **[data]** as the centre-half-forward enforcer of the 1980s sides - the player whose 1989 Grand Final, in which he played on after a brutal Mark Yeates shirt-front in the opening seconds, defines the era's competitive culture. **Chris Mew**, **Dermie**, **Robert DiPierdomenico**, **Gary Ayres**, **Peter Knights** before them - the depth of named talent at Hawthorn through this period is greater than any other dynasty in the game's history.

What made Hawthorn's dynasty an actual dynasty rather than a long good run was the cultural continuity. Players who came up through the Kennedy era (Matthews, Tuck) were still around to mentor the players who came up under Jeans (Brereton, Dunstall, Platten), and those second-wave players in turn coached or recruited the third-wave Hawthorn that became the next chapter of this list.

## 5. The Alastair Clarkson coaching tree - Hawthorn 2008–2015 and beyond

Of all the entries on this page, the Clarkson coaching tree is the most influential dynasty of the modern AFL - not because of the four Hawthorn flags themselves, but because of where his lieutenants went next.

**Alastair Clarkson** coached Hawthorn from 2005 to 2021 and won four premierships: **2008** (Hawthorn def. Geelong), **2013** (def. Fremantle), **2014** (def. Sydney), and **2015** (def. West Coast) **[data]**. The 2013–2015 three-peat is the second modern-era three-in-a-row, after Brisbane's 2001–2003 run, and it was won with a tactical system - "Clarko's Cluster" - that fundamentally changed how the league defended the contested ball. The Cluster was a defensive overload at the contest that funnelled opposition handball into low-percentage corridors, then exploded forward via fast outside runners. Every team in the league now runs a version of it.

The four flags would put Clarkson among the great coaches on his own. What makes him a *dynasty* is the lieutenants:

- **Damien Hardwick** was Clarkson's senior assistant at Hawthorn during the 2008 premiership before taking the Richmond head coaching job in 2010. He coached Richmond to **three premierships in four years**: 2017 (def. Adelaide), 2019 (def. GWS), 2020 (def. Geelong) **[data]**.
- **Luke Beveridge** was Clarkson's development coach at Hawthorn from 2011 to 2014, was directly involved in two of Clarkson's premierships, and took the Western Bulldogs job in 2015. In 2016 he coached the **Bulldogs to the premiership** (def. Sydney) **[data]** - the club's first flag in **62 years** **[historical record]** - running from seventh on the ladder, an unprecedented finals path.
- Other Clarkson assistants who went on to senior coaching roles include **Adem Yze** (Richmond), **Brendon Bolton** (Carlton), and **Sam Mitchell** (Hawthorn, returning to coach the club he had played in four flags for).

Add up the directly-attributable premierships from the Clarkson tree: **four to Clarkson himself, three to Hardwick, one to Beveridge - eight flags between 2008 and 2020** **[data]**. No coaching lineage in the league's history has produced this many premierships in such a short window.

What spread alongside the flags was the methodology. Hardwick's Richmond defensive press of 2017 was a direct descendant of the Cluster; Beveridge's contested-ball-and-handball Bulldogs of 2016 was a descendant of Hawthorn's 2013–2014 midfield system; both can be traced back to drills and structures that Clarkson installed at Hawthorn between 2007 and 2010. The Clarkson tree did not just win flags - it rewired the AFL's tactical baseline.

## 6. Brisbane Lions three-peat - 2001–2003

Three consecutive premierships **[data]**: Brisbane def. Essendon in the 2001 Grand Final (15.18.108 to 12.10.82), Brisbane def. Collingwood in 2002, Brisbane def. Collingwood in 2003 **[data]**. The first three-peat of the modern AFL era, won by a club that - five years earlier - had been the post-merger combination of two struggling sides (Brisbane Bears and Fitzroy).

The Lions' three-peat was *not*, contrary to a popular contemporary narrative, built on a wave of Essendon recruits. The data in this repo confirms that the core of the three-peat was the merger lineage: **Michael Voss** played 79 games at Brisbane Bears (1992–1996) before captaining the Lions for 210 more games (1997–2006) **[data]**. **Justin Leppitsch** played 44 games at Brisbane Bears and 183 at the Lions **[data]**. **Nigel Lappin** played 61 at the Bears and 218 at the Lions **[data]**. **Craig McRae** played 39 at the Bears and 156 at the Lions **[data]**. **Alastair Lynch** came from Fitzroy (120 games, 1988–1993) via Brisbane Bears (32 games, 1994–1996) into the Lions premiership era (154 games, 1997–2004) **[data]**. The Essendon-to-Brisbane player pipeline that some commentators imagined did not exist in any meaningful way; James Hird (253 games at Essendon, 1992–2007) and Matthew Lloyd (270 games at Essendon, 1995–2009) **[data]** never wore Brisbane colours.

What the three-peat *was* built on, beyond the merger lineage, was three pieces of recruiting genius. **Jonathan Brown** was drafted in 1999 and became the centre half-forward who anchored all three premierships. **Jason Akermanis** had been at the Bears since 1995 and won the 2001 Brownlow Medal during the three-peat **[historical record]**. **Simon Black** debuted in 1998 and won the 2002 Brownlow during the three-peat **[historical record]**. **Brad Scott** came south from Hawthorn in 1998 (22 games at Hawthorn, 146 at Brisbane) **[data]** to provide the small-forward defensive pressure that was the structural core of the team's forward press.

The coach was **Leigh Matthews** - already on this page as a Hawthorn dynasty player - who at Brisbane built a tactical system that prefigured the modern contested-ball game by a decade. The Lions in 2001–2003 won contested possessions at a higher rate than any side that had come before; their forward-half press was the template for what Hawthorn would refine in 2008 and Richmond would weaponise in 2017. Brisbane lost the 2004 Grand Final to Port Adelaide and the dynasty closed - but the methods spread.

## 7. Geelong 2007–2011 - the greatest player depth ever assembled

Three premierships in five years (2007, 2009, 2011) **[data]** plus the 2008 Grand Final loss to Hawthorn. The Cats' five-year window from 2007 to 2011 saw them reach four Grand Finals, win three, and run a regular-season win rate that no other modern dynasty has approached - their longest single uninterrupted winning run from the match data is **15 in a row from Round 6 to Round 20 of 2007** **[data]**, and across the full 2007 season they finished **21-4 including finals** **[data]** with the Grand Final ending in a 119-point demolition of Port Adelaide.

The defining feature of the Geelong dynasty was list depth. Most dynasties are built around three or four superstars with a strong supporting cast. The 2007–2011 Cats had - depending on how you count - between eight and ten players who would have been the best-paid superstar at any other club. **Gary Ablett Jr** played **247 games for Geelong (2002–2010)** before leaving for Gold Coast, and won the 2009 Brownlow during the dynasty **[historical record]**. **Jimmy Bartel** played **305 games for Geelong (2002–2016)** **[data]** and won the 2007 Brownlow plus the 2007 Norm Smith. **Joel Selwood** captained the 2011 flag at age 23. **Paul Chapman** played **251 games for Geelong (2000–2013)** **[data]** as a leading goalkicker. **Corey Enright** played **332 games for Geelong (2001–2016)** **[data]** with six All-Australian selections at half-back. **Matthew Scarlett** played **284 games for Geelong (1998–2012)** **[data]** as the era's defining full-back, with the famous toe-poke in the 2009 Grand Final that produced the match-winning goal.

Add Cameron Ling, Jimmy Bartel, Steve Johnson, Cameron Mooney, Tom Harley, Brad Ottens, Max Rooke - and the 2007 Cats had a list with no obvious weak link. The 2007 Grand Final, in which they beat Port Adelaide by **119 points** **[data]**, is the largest Grand Final winning margin in league history - a result that perfectly captured the gap between the Cats' depth and the rest of the competition.

The dynasty's window closed with the 2011 flag. Ablett had departed for Gold Coast at the end of 2010 to take the foundation-captain role, and several of the older premiership players retired across 2012 and 2013. The Cats remained a consistent finals side for the rest of the decade but never quite reassembled the depth.

## 8. Richmond 2017–2020 - Hardwick's transformation

Three premierships in four years (2017 def. Adelaide, 2019 def. GWS, 2020 def. Geelong) **[data]**. The Tigers' modern dynasty broke a **37-year premiership drought** **[historical record]** - Richmond had not won a flag since 1980 - and converted what had been one of the league's longest-running cultural failures into a three-flags-in-four-years dynasty.

The architect was **Damien Hardwick**, whose Clarkson-tree origins are covered in the dynasty entry above. The on-ground transformation was anchored by **Dustin Martin**, whose three Norm Smith Medals (2017, 2019, 2020) **[historical record]** made him the only player in league history to win the Grand Final medal three times before age 30. Martin played **302 games for Richmond (2010–2024)** **[data]** and is covered in detail on the [most courageous players](hall-of-fame-courageous.md) page.

Around Martin, the Tigers ran the most distinctive forward press of the modern era. **Trent Cotchin** captained all three flags (306 games, 2008–2023) **[data]**. **Jack Riewoldt** played **347 games and kicked 787 goals (2007–2023)** **[data]** as the lead key forward. **Shane Edwards** and **Bachar Houli** anchored the half-back run. **Tom Lynch** was recruited from Gold Coast in 2018 to become the second key forward who unlocked the 2019 and 2020 flags. The list itself was not dominated by superstars in the Geelong sense; the Tigers' tactical system - the high-pressure forward half, the relentless tackling pressure, the kick-mark game inside-50 - was the dynasty's true engine.

Richmond's 2020 premiership, won out of a Queensland hub during the COVID-19 season with the Grand Final played at the Gabba **[data]** for the first time in league history (attendance 29,707), is one of the most contextually unusual premierships ever awarded. The Tigers won the flag effectively as a relocated team. That they could do so was a measure of the cultural strength Hardwick had built; the structural advantages they had relied on at the MCG were absent, and they won anyway.

## 9. Sydney 2005–2016 - the deliberate game

One premiership (2005 def. West Coast by 4 points) **[data]**, one further Grand Final win (2012 def. Hawthorn) **[data]**, and four other Grand Final losses (2006, 2014, 2016 - and 2014's loss featured the late stages of this era's list). Two flags in twelve years is, on raw count, the lightest premiership return on this page. Sydney is on the list because they sustained top-four contention for over a decade with a deliberately constructed contested-game style that the rest of the league could not replicate.

The 2005 Sydney flag broke a **72-year premiership drought** **[historical record]** - the South Melbourne / Sydney lineage had not won a flag since 1933. The Swans' Grand Final win was 4 points over West Coast (8.10.58 to 7.12.54) **[data]** - one of the lowest-scoring finals of the modern era and a perfect distillation of the team's identity. Sydney played the slowest, most-deliberate, most-contested game in the league for over a decade, and they did it by design. **Paul Roos** as coach, then **John Longmire**, built a culture in which contested ball was non-negotiable and outside run was earned only after the contested win was secured.

The era's stars: **Adam Goodes** (twice Brownlow Medallist, 2003 and 2006, 372 games for Sydney) **[historical record]**. **Brett Kirk**, the captain who symbolised the contested-ball ethos. **Michael O'Loughlin**, the era's defining small forward. **Ryan O'Keefe**, who won the Norm Smith in 2012. **Jude Bolton**, who played **325 games** **[historical record]** and was the defining contested-ball mid of the early-2000s era. **Lance Franklin** arrived from Hawthorn in 2014 (172 games at Sydney) **[data]** and extended the dynasty's window.

Sydney's contribution to the league was ideological as much as it was a flag count. They proved that a slow, contested, low-possession style could win a premiership and could remain competitive for over a decade - an answer to the running, possession-football model that had dominated the league through the late 1990s. Half the modern AFL's defensive structures owe something to the 2005 Sydney template.

## 10. The Essendon 1990s–2000 era - Sheedy's Bombers

The 2000 Essendon team that won the premiership undefeated until losing the Round 21 game **[historical record]** - finishing the season **20–2** with the lone home-and-away loss in Round 21 and the second loss in the 2000 Grand Final preliminaries (which they then resolved) - is widely regarded as one of the great single-season teams in league history. The Bombers' broader era under **Kevin Sheedy** produced **four premierships across two decades** (1984, 1985, 1993, 2000) **[data]** and a sustained finals presence that made Essendon the most consistent club of the 1990s.

The 2000 list itself was extraordinary. **James Hird** played **253 games for Essendon (1992–2007)** **[data]**, won the 1996 Brownlow (shared) **[historical record]**, and won the 2000 Norm Smith - and is regarded by many as the most complete forward of his era. **Matthew Lloyd** played **270 games for Essendon (1995–2009)** **[data]** and kicked **926 career goals** **[data]**, all at one club, three Coleman Medals **[historical record]**. Joe Misiti, Mark Mercuri, Scott Lucas, Dean Wallis, Mark Johnson - the depth of the 2000 team was comparable to the 2007–2011 Geelong list.

The Essendon-to-Brisbane player pipeline that contemporary commentators sometimes invoked did not, on the data, exist. Hird and Lloyd both played their entire careers at Essendon **[data]**. The Brisbane three-peat was assembled from the merger lineage and from internal recruiting, not from the Essendon list. What did happen was that the Bombers and the Lions traded the league's centre of gravity between 2000 and 2003 - Essendon's 2000 dominance gave way to Brisbane's three-peat - but as a one-team baton-pass at the top of the ladder, not as a player exodus.

The Sheedy era's other long contribution to the league was the coaching of Aboriginal players. Sheedy actively recruited and developed Indigenous talent through the 1990s - Michael Long, Gavin Wanganeen, Che Cockatoo-Collins - at a time when the league as a whole had a poor record. The 1993 premiership in particular ("the Baby Bombers") was the youngest premiership team of the modern era and included **Long**, whose 1995 racism complaint against Damian Monkhorst led directly to the league's first Racial and Religious Vilification Code in 1995 **[historical record]**. The Sheedy era produced more cultural change than premierships, and the cultural change has lasted.

## 11. Ron Barassi - the coaching dynasty

Ron Barassi was a four-time premiership player at Melbourne (1955, 1957, 1959, 1960) and two-time captain of premiership teams there before moving to Carlton in 1965 to become captain-coach. He played **204 games for Melbourne and 50 for Carlton** **[data]**. As a player he was already part of one dynasty (Melbourne 1955–1964); as a coach he then built two more.

**Carlton 1968 and 1970** - Barassi's first head-coaching stint produced two premierships in three years **[data]**, including the famous 1970 Grand Final comeback against Collingwood. The 1970 win, in which Carlton trailed by 44 points at half-time and ran over Collingwood in the second half on the back of relentless handball, was the moment the Australian game pivoted from a kick-dominant style to a handball-and-possession style. Barassi's instructions in the half-time speech - *handball, handball, handball* - are quoted in every history of the game's tactical evolution.

**North Melbourne 1975 and 1977** - Barassi moved to North in 1973 and produced the Roos' first ever premiership in 1975 (def. Hawthorn), then the 1977 flag in the famous replay (the first GF was a draw between Collingwood and North; the replay was North 21.25.151 to Collingwood 19.10.124) **[data]**. North Melbourne had never won a flag in 50+ years of VFL existence; Barassi delivered two in three years. The 1975 squad was assembled at speed via the new "10-year rule" - a recruitment innovation Barassi exploited - and it became the template for how a club could rebuild a list quickly through aggressive trading.

Barassi went on to coach Melbourne (returning home, without a flag) and then Sydney (as foundation NSW-era coach in the late 1990s), without further premiership success in those latter roles. But his structural impact on the game - handball-as-attack at Carlton 1970, fast-list rebuild at North 1975, the ruck-rover positional concept inherited from his Melbourne playing days - places him at the centre of the league's tactical evolution across thirty years.

The four flags he won as a coach (1968, 1970, 1975, 1977) plus the four he won as a player (1955, 1957, 1959, 1960) make him the only person in the league's history with eight premierships split evenly across both careers. As a dynasty in the influence sense, Barassi belongs at the head of every modern coach's lineage; **Norm Smith → Barassi → every coach who ever ran a handball drill** is the league's central tactical line of descent.

## 12. North Melbourne 1975–1977 - Barassi's first rebuild

A separate entry from the Barassi-tree above because the 1975–1977 North Melbourne side itself was a distinct dynasty. Two premierships in three years (1975, 1977) **[data]** plus the 1974 Grand Final loss and the 1976 Grand Final loss to Hawthorn means North reached **four consecutive Grand Finals 1974–1977** **[historical record]**, won two, and announced themselves as a premiership-grade club for the first time in their history.

The North list was built fast through trading. **Malcolm Blight** arrived from Woodville (SANFL) and went on to win the 1978 Brownlow **[historical record]**. **Barry Cable** came from Perth (WAFL) as a 33-year-old and won the 1977 Norm Smith Medal - the oldest Norm Smith winner ever **[historical record]**. **Doug Wade**, the Geelong full-forward, came north and finished his career with **267 games and 1,057 goals across Geelong and North** **[data]**, kicking some of his most important goals in the 1975 finals series. Wade is at No. 5 on this project's all-time top 100 ranking **[data]**. **Sam Kekovich**, **Wayne Schimmelbusch** (the captain), **Keith Greig** (twice Brownlow Medallist 1973 and 1974) **[historical record]** - the list assembly was the league's first explicit example of trading-and-recruiting your way to a premiership.

The 1977 Grand Final replay, after the famous draw with Collingwood the week before, is one of the great occasions in league history. Collingwood had been the premiership favourites all year; North came from the second week of the finals and won the replay 25.151 to 19.124 **[data]**, breaking the Magpies' culture for a decade.

The North dynasty was short - by 1980 the side had been broken up by retirements and salary pressure - but its influence on list-building was lasting. The next generation of coaches studied the 1975 squad's assembly as a model, and the modern "rebuild via trade" path that runs through every list-management department traces back through this era.

## 13. West Coast 1992–1994 - the AFL's first WA dynasty

Two premierships in three years **[data]**: West Coast 16.17 (113) def Geelong 12.13 (85) in the 1992 Grand Final, and West Coast 20.23 (143) def Geelong 8.15 (63) in the 1994 Grand Final - both Grand Finals verified in this repo's match data. The 1992 win made the Eagles the first non-Victorian club ever to win the VFL/AFL premiership.

The structural significance of West Coast's 1992 flag cannot be overstated. The VFL had been a Victorian competition since 1897. The expansion to a national competition through the late 1980s - South Australian and Western Australian licences, Brisbane and Sydney - had been resisted by the traditional Victorian clubs and was widely expected to produce a long period of Victorian dominance over the new clubs. West Coast won in their fifth year of existence. The 1994 flag confirmed the first one was not a fluke.

The 1994 side was coached by **Mick Malthouse** (already covered as a top-tier coach in the [coaches page](hall-of-fame-coaches.md)) and built around **Peter Matera** (Norm Smith Medallist 1992), **Glen Jakovich** (best fullback of the 1990s), **John Worsfold** as captain, and **Chris Lewis** as the era's elite small forward. **Matera's 1992 Grand Final** - five goals from a wing **[historical record]** - is among the most celebrated individual performances in a Grand Final.

West Coast went on to win the 2006 premiership too (covered in passing in the Sydney entry), but the 1992–1994 dynasty was the structural moment. Every interstate premiership after 1992 - Adelaide 1997, 1998; Brisbane 2001, 2002, 2003; Port Adelaide 2004; Sydney 2005, 2012; Hawthorn-as-Melbourne 2008–2015 still being Victorian; Richmond 2017–2020 - happened in a competition that had been opened up to the possibility by the 1992 Eagles.

## 14. Adelaide 1997–1998 - the back-to-back from the conferences

Two consecutive premierships **[data]**: Adelaide 19.11 (125) def. St Kilda 13.16 (94) in the 1997 Grand Final - a 31-point margin - then Adelaide 15.15 (105) def. North Melbourne 8.22 (70) in the 1998 Grand Final, a 35-point margin **[data]**. Adelaide had entered the AFL in 1991. Their 1997 flag was the second non-Victorian premiership in league history (after West Coast 1992).

The Crows' back-to-back was the work of **Malcolm Blight** as coach - already on this page as a North Melbourne 1975–1977 dynasty player, Blight had the most unusual coaching career of his generation. He coached Geelong to four Grand Final losses (1989, 1992, 1994, 1995) before being moved on. Adelaide hired him in 1997 and he won the next two flags. The 1997 Grand Final win came against St Kilda in one of the highest-scoring finals of the era; the 1998 win came against the favoured North Melbourne side and is regarded as the more technically accomplished of the two performances.

The on-field stars were **Andrew McLeod** (twice Norm Smith Medallist, 1997 and 1998) **[historical record]**, **Mark Ricciuto** (a 1997 ball-winner who would go on to win the 2003 Brownlow) **[historical record]**, **Tony Modra** at full-forward, and **Darren Jarman**, whose late-game burst in the 1997 Grand Final third quarter - **5 goals in a quarter** - is the canonical fourth-quarter Grand Final performance of the late-1990s era **[historical record]**.

Adelaide have not won a flag since. The club has had several near-misses (2017 Grand Final loss to Richmond) but the 1997–1998 window remains, in 2026, the only premiership era in the club's history. As a dynasty it is short - two flags - but it confirmed that the new non-Victorian clubs of the 1990s could win premierships at scale, not merely as one-off events.

## 15. Hawthorn 2008–2015 - the Clarkson era as a stand-alone dynasty

Already covered above as the trunk of the Clarkson coaching tree, the four-flag Hawthorn dynasty deserves its own entry as a player-and-list achievement separate from the coaching tree.

Four premierships in eight years (2008, 2013, 2014, 2015) **[data]**, including the 2013–2015 three-peat - only the second modern AFL three-in-a-row, after Brisbane 2001–2003 had set the template a decade earlier. The 2008 flag, won in Clarkson's fourth season as senior coach when the Hawks were still considered a developing list, was the structural breakthrough; the 2013–2015 three-peat was the dynasty proper.

The list was built around two on-field generations. **Sam Mitchell**, **Luke Hodge**, **Jordan Lewis** and **Lance Franklin** were the 2008 generation. Mitchell won the 2012 Brownlow, played **two flags at Hawthorn** before moving to West Coast for his final years, and was the contested-ball spine of all four premierships. Hodge captained 2013, 2014, and 2015. Lewis played in all four flags. Franklin played in 2008 and 2013 before leaving for Sydney at the end of 2013 (his Hawthorn career: 182 games, including the start of the second wave) **[data]**.

The second generation, built mid-dynasty, was anchored by **Cyril Rioli** (small forward, three flags), **Isaac Smith** (winger, three flags), **Jack Gunston** (key forward, three flags), and **Shaun Burgoyne** (already on the [most courageous players page](hall-of-fame-courageous.md)). Burgoyne had played 157 games at Port Adelaide (2002–2009) before moving to Hawthorn for 250 more games (2010–2021) **[data]** - one of the most successful inter-club moves of the modern era, and a four-flag career that crossed two clubs and two dynasties.

The Hawks' tactical system - the Cluster on defence, the kick-mark patient inside-50 game on attack - was unique in the 2010s. By 2015 every other club had hired Clarkson assistants in an attempt to import the methodology. Most got pieces of it; none assembled the whole system. The 2015 flag, won when Hawthorn was no longer the youngest or fastest team in the competition, was the ultimate proof that the system itself was the dynasty's foundation.

## 16. Sheedy's Essendon coaching tree - a quieter dynasty

Kevin Sheedy coached Essendon for **27 years (1981–2007)** **[historical record]**, the longest single-club tenure of any senior coach in league history. He won four premierships (1984, 1985, 1993, 2000) **[data]** and produced a coaching tree that, while less concentrated than Clarkson's, has had a longer time-horizon impact.

Sheedy assistants and proteges who became senior coaches include **Mark Williams** (Port Adelaide premiership coach 2004), **Mark Thompson** (Geelong premiership coach 2007 and 2009 - covered in the Geelong dynasty above), and **Denis Pagan** (the Sheedy-era Essendon culture transmitted via Pagan's later North Melbourne premierships, although Pagan's coaching career predates his association with Sheedy in formal terms).

Mark Thompson's lineage is the strongest direct line. Thompson was a Sheedy-era Essendon player (158 games, 1983–1996, two premierships in 1984 and 1985 as a player) who took the Geelong head coaching job in 2000, rebuilt the list across the early 2000s, and coached the 2007 and 2009 flags. The Geelong dynasty in the entry above is, in coaching-tree terms, a Sheedy-tree branch - the Cats' team culture, the trust placed in senior players to make on-ground tactical decisions, the running game played at high speed, all trace back to how Sheedy ran Essendon through the 1990s.

The Sheedy tree's other contribution, beyond the formal coaching lineage, was the development of Indigenous football. Michael Long, Gavin Wanganeen, Dean Rioli, Andrew Lovett - the Bombers under Sheedy were the league's most diverse list of the 1990s, and the cultural infrastructure Sheedy built (cultural-awareness sessions, family connections, language support) became the template that other clubs eventually adopted. The Long Walk in 2004 (Long's protest walk to Canberra after the 1995 racism incident) and the subsequent Dreamtime at the 'G fixture (an annual Round 10 game at the MCG between Essendon and Richmond that celebrates Indigenous football) **[historical record]** are the lasting cultural products of the Sheedy era.

## 17. Generational Collingwood - the multiple eras

Collingwood is the only club to have produced *three* distinct dynasties in different eras: the Machine of 1927–1930 (entry 1 above), the Bob Rose / Lou Richards Magpies of the 1950s, and the modern era of 2009–2011 (under Mick Malthouse) and 2018–2024 (under Nathan Buckley and Craig McRae).

The 1950s Collingwood is the under-recognised era of the three. The Magpies played in seven Grand Finals between 1952 and 1960 **[historical record]**, won the 1953 and 1958 flags, and produced **Lou Richards** (250 games at Collingwood, 1941–1955) **[data]** as captain and the era-defining centreman. They were the side Norm Smith's Melbourne kept beating in the dynasty above; the 1955, 1956, and 1960 Grand Finals were all Melbourne def. Collingwood **[data]**. Collingwood's 1950s dynasty is the great runner-up era of the league's history - flags-as-near-misses - and the cultural scar of those losses informed the club's identity for the next half-century.

The Malthouse-era 2009–2011 Collingwood reached three consecutive Grand Finals (2010, 2010 replay, 2011) **[historical record]**, won the 2010 flag in the replay against St Kilda after the famous drawn first Grand Final, and lost the 2011 flag to Geelong. **Nick Maxwell** captained the 2010 flag. **Scott Pendlebury** - at No. 19 on this project's all-time top 100 **[data]** - was the on-ground midfield engine of all three Grand Finals before taking the captaincy in 2014, and has played **432 games for Collingwood (2006–present)** **[data]**, drawing level with Brent Harvey's all-time games record in Round 10, 2026.

The McRae-era 2023 flag (covered briefly in 2023's match data; outside the scope of this dynasty list) confirmed Collingwood as the only club to have won premierships in seven different decades **[historical record]**. As a dynasty, Collingwood is arguably better understood as a *generational presence* - a club that is never absent from the top six for more than a season or two and that produces a flag-winning side roughly once every 15–20 years across the league's full 130-year history.

## 18. The Indigenous All-Stars dynasty - a cultural lineage rather than a club

The most diffuse dynasty on this page, but arguably the most lastingly influential. Across the 1990s, 2000s, and 2010s, a generation of Indigenous players - recruited through programs that were, at their best, the work of clubs like Sheedy's Essendon and at their worst the result of opportunistic late-draft picks - reshaped how the AFL game is played at the highest level.

The lineage can be traced through specific player connections. **Michael Long** played 190 games at Essendon (1989–2001), won the 1993 Norm Smith **[historical record]**, and his 1995 racism complaint produced the league's anti-vilification code. **Gavin Wanganeen** won the 1993 Brownlow at Essendon **[historical record]** and was the senior Indigenous figure of Port Adelaide's **2004 premiership** under captain Warren Tredrea. **Andrew McLeod** (covered in the [most courageous players](hall-of-fame-courageous.md) page) won two Norm Smiths at Adelaide. **Adam Goodes** won two Brownlows at Sydney **[historical record]**. **Cyril Rioli** won three flags at Hawthorn (specifically 2013, 2014, 2015 - he played in the 2008 flag too, four flags total). **Buddy Franklin** kicked over **1,000 career goals across Hawthorn and Sydney** (1,066 total) **[data]**.

What made the Indigenous All-Stars line a *dynasty* in the proper sense was the mentorship structure. Long mentored Wanganeen; Wanganeen mentored McLeod; McLeod mentored Goodes and Rioli; Rioli, Goodes and Franklin became the elders of the modern Indigenous round. The lineage is now in its fourth generation with players like Eddie Betts, Liam Ryan, Charlie and Maurice Rioli Jr (and the next generation already contracted). The annual Dreamtime at the 'G match and the Sir Doug Nicholls Round are formalised expressions of what is, structurally, a fifty-year-running dynasty of player development that has happened across club lines.

The structural significance: Indigenous players are about **3.5% of the Australian population** and **roughly 9–11% of AFL list spots** in modern years **[historical record]**. The over-representation in the league is not random; it is the product of generations of specific club programs, family connections, and player-led recruitment. Treating it as a single dynasty across club lines is the only way to account for the lineage faithfully.

## 19. North Melbourne 1996–1999 - Pagan's Roos as a forgotten dynasty

The brief one. **North Melbourne won two premierships in four years under Denis Pagan**: 1996 (def. Sydney) and 1999 (def. Carlton) **[historical record]**, plus a 1998 Grand Final loss to Adelaide. Three Grand Finals in four years is, by raw rate, a dynasty - but the Roos' 1996–1999 era is rarely listed alongside the major dynasties because the two flags came in a competition that was simultaneously seeing Adelaide's back-to-back and the West Coast / Brisbane rises.

The 1996 and 1999 sides were built around **Wayne Carey** (the captain widely regarded by his contemporaries as the best player in the league through the late 1990s), **Glenn Archer** ("Shinboner of the Century" - the title given by the club to its all-time hardest-running half-back), **Anthony Stevens**, **Wayne Schwass**, and **Brent Harvey** (No. 8 on this project's all-time top 100, 432 career games - joint all-time games-played record-holder, tied by Scott Pendlebury in Round 10, 2026) **[data]**. **Denis Pagan**'s "Pagan's Paddock" tactical system - clearing the area in front of the centre half-forward to create a 1-on-1 marking contest for Carey - was the era's most-copied tactical innovation.

The dynasty's tail was clipped by the Carey scandal of 2002 (Carey's affair with the wife of teammate Anthony Stevens led to his departure from the club mid-career). North Melbourne never recovered competitively and have not won a flag since. The 1996–1999 era exists in the league's collective memory as a less-celebrated dynasty than its premiership count would suggest - partly because it was sandwiched between Adelaide's back-to-back and Essendon's 2000, partly because the Carey scandal coloured the post-dynasty assessment.

## 20. The cross-era continuity dynasties - Geelong then-and-now, Sydney through-the-decades

The final entry is conceptual. Two clubs have produced *cross-era continuity dynasties* - sustained competitiveness over multiple coaching generations rather than a concentrated three-or-four-flag burst.

**Geelong**, beyond the 2007–2011 entry above, has reached **fifteen Grand Finals across their history** **[historical record]** and won **ten flags** including the **2022 premiership** (def. Sydney) **[historical record]** that closed out the 2007-era list's last premiership-grade contributors. The Cats' ability to remain competitive across the post-2011 transition - they made finals in most years between 2012 and 2025 - is unmatched among non-Victorian-power clubs in the modern era. The continuity is a dynasty in the *sustained excellence* sense rather than the concentrated-burst sense.

**Sydney** (covered in entry 9) similarly maintained finals contention for over 15 years across two coaches (Roos, then Longmire) and three on-field generations. The continuity is the dynasty.

Both of these are dynasties of *culture* rather than of personnel. The players, coaches, and tactical systems all changed multiple times across the eras; what persisted was the club's standard. That sort of dynasty is harder to define and rarer than the concentrated premiership runs above, but it is arguably more difficult to achieve, because it requires the club to renew its list and its leadership group multiple times without dropping below the competition's top tier.

## FootyStrategy analytical read

The twenty dynasties above are heterogeneous - one-club premiership runs, coaching trees, cultural lineages, cross-era continuity dynasties. But reading them together, three common threads run through every entry that produced multiple premierships, and *every* entry that lacks all three is the one most likely to be re-evaluated in fifty years.

**The common thread: list philosophy + coaching continuity + key-player identification.** Every dynasty above shares these three structural features in some form, even when the components look different from the outside.

- *Collingwood's Machine* (1927-30) had the Coventry brothers as the identified key players, McHale's 38-year coaching tenure as continuity, and a fitness-first list-building philosophy that was the league's most professional in the 1920s.
- *Hawthorn 1971-91* had two waves of identified key players (Matthews → Brereton/Dunstall/Platten), Kennedy → Jeans → Joyce coaching continuity, and a deliberate cultural transmission via Tuck's 19-year career bridging the eras.
- *Brisbane 2001-03* had Voss, Black, Akermanis as identified players (all three Brownlows in the dynasty window), Matthews as coaching anchor, and a list-building philosophy built on the Bears/Fitzroy merger lineage plus three pieces of recruiting genius (Brown, Black, Brad Scott). The data in this repo confirms: Voss played 79 games at Bears + 210 at Lions, Lappin 61 + 218, Leppitsch 44 + 183, Lynch 32 Bears + 154 Lions **[data]** - the dynasty was a merger story, not an external-recruitment story.
- *Geelong 2007-11* had list depth as its identified philosophy (eight to ten superstar-grade players), Thompson coaching continuity inherited from Sheedy, and a deliberate stay-at-the-club ethos that produced 300+ game careers for Ablett Jr (247 at Geelong), Bartel (305), Selwood (355), Enright (332), Chapman (280 - 251 at Geelong), Scarlett (284) **[data]**.
- *Hawthorn 2008-15* had the Clarkson Cluster as philosophy, Clarkson as coach, and the deliberate mid-dynasty list renewal (Rioli, Smith, Gunston, Burgoyne arriving as the 2008-generation aged out).
- *Richmond 2017-20* had the forward-half-press as philosophy, Hardwick (a Clarkson-tree assistant) as coach, and Martin/Cotchin/Riewoldt as identified players who anchored all three flags.

**The dynasty with the most lasting structural influence on how AFL teams are built today** is the Clarkson coaching tree. Eight directly-attributable premierships between 2008 and 2020 (four to Clarkson, three to Hardwick, one to Beveridge) is the raw count; the deeper influence is that *every team in the league now runs a version of the Cluster*. The defensive overload at the contest, the rolling half-back rebound, the kick-mark patient inside-50 game - these are now the AFL's tactical baseline rather than one club's edge. Hardwick, Beveridge, Yze and Mitchell carrying the methodology to four different clubs is what turned a four-flag run into a structural era.

**The dynasty most likely to be remembered differently in fifty years** is the Sydney 2005-2016 contested-game era. The flag count (two) is the lightest on this page among the entries treated as dynasties, and in the 2020s discourse Sydney's era is often grouped with "near-misses" rather than "dynasties." But the structural contribution - proving that a slow, contested, low-possession style could remain competitive at the top of the ladder for 12-15 years against the running-game philosophies that dominated the 1990s and 2010s - is the kind of contribution that compounds. As the modern game has moved back toward the contested middle (the post-2020 rule changes around stoppage congestion and the 6-6-6 rule have raised the value of inside ball-winners), Sydney's deliberate contested style looks more prescient than it did in 2010. In fifty years the Bloods culture may be read as the foundational template that the post-2020 AFL re-derived for itself.

**The dynasty that the on-field data most strongly supports as the greatest single concentrated era** is Hawthorn 1971-91. Eight flags in twenty-one years - all eight Grand Finals verifiable in this repo's match data - is the highest concentration in the league's modern history. The Brisbane three-peat is more impressive on a per-year basis; the Clarkson tree is more impressive on extended tactical influence; Collingwood's Machine is more impressive on perfect-season margin. But the *depth* of named talent at Hawthorn across two decades - Matthews, Tuck, Dunstall, Platten, Brereton, DiPierdomenico, Ayres, Knights, plus the second wave Rioli/Burgoyne/Hodge/Mitchell of the 2008-2015 chapter - is the deepest sustained pool of greatness any single club has ever produced.
