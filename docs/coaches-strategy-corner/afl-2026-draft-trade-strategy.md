**Reading the table:**
- **Elite %** = the share of a club's National Draft picks who went on to an A or A+ career (DraftGuru grade). League average is 10.1%.
- **Avg pick** = the average pick position a club uses. Lower = using earlier picks (more assets spent). Higher = finding value deeper in the draft.
- **Avg games/pick** = average AFL career games across all that club's tracked picks, including busts. Measures total return per asset expended.

### Brisbane Lions

**Draft efficiency: 6.0% elite — 2nd lowest in the competition** **[data]**

**[data]** 100 tracked picks 2004–2025. 6 elite players (1 A+, 5 A). Average pick 36.4. Average career 69.9 games per pick.

**Elite picks from National Draft:** **[data]**

| Player | Year | Pick | Grade | Games |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Harris Andrews | 2014 | 61 | A+ | 250 |
| Hugh McCluggage | 2016 | 3 | A | 218 |
| Daniel Rich | 2008 | 7 | A | 275 |
| Zac Bailey | 2017 | 15 | A | 179 |
| Elliot Yeo | 2011 | 30 | A | 224 |
| Sam Docherty | 2011 | 12 | A | 184 |

**The 6.0% efficiency number requires context:** Brisbane have taken the most picks of any club in this window (100) — the Lions held compensation picks and expansion assets through their rebuilding era. Volume of picks inflates the denominator and pushes the elite rate down. But the rate itself is still the second worst in the competition; it is not purely an artefact of pick volume.

**Retention losses are the core story:** Elliot Yeo (2011 pick 30, 224 games) is counted as a Brisbane pick but played his elite career at West Coast. Sam Docherty (2011 pick 12, 184 games) played his career at Carlton. Two of their six elite picks from this era delivered their value elsewhere. The Lions' draft operation finds players; the retention and development operation has historically failed to keep them.

**The trade success that masks the draft:** Joe Daniher (Essendon), Eric Hipwood (homegrown via father-son), Cam Rayner (pick 1 2017 — rookie delisted and redrafted by Brisbane) and Charlie Cameron (Gold Coast) are the backbone of Brisbane's premiership core — all acquired outside the National Draft or through the rookie/father-son pathway. The 6% national draft rate is the honest number; the premiership window was built on trade-table competence, not draft excellence.

**Current pipeline and what they need:** The data shows zero A+ picks and only five A picks across 100 national draft selections. The next contention window requires that the 2020–2025 cohort (Deven Robertson, Will Ashcroft, Cam Rayner, Harry Sharp) produces multiple A-grades. Ashcroft is the key: pick 2 in 2021, currently ungraded due to games count. If he grades A+, the story changes. Otherwise, Brisbane must again build through the trade table.

**The Geelong paradox in one number:** Average pick 42.0. That is lower than St Kilda's (41.2) despite St Kilda being the worst efficiency club in the competition. **The trade model layered on top:** This dataset credits Geelong for the draft picks only. The 2020–2025 draft cohort (Holmes, Clark, Max Michalanney, Ollie Dempsey) must produce A/A+ careers to maintain the model. If they don't, the trade-import model requires paying premium prices for players from a position of list weakness rather than list depth.

## Summary: what the data says

**Above league average (>10.1%):** Western Bulldogs, GWS, Collingwood, Adelaide, Melbourne, Sydney, West Coast, Geelong — these eight clubs have demonstrated the ability to find and develop elite talent from the National Draft at a higher rate than the competition. Historical efficiency does not guarantee future performance, but it is the best predictor available.

**Below league average (<10.1%):** Fremantle, Carlton, Essendon, Richmond, Port Adelaide, Gold Coast, Brisbane, St Kilda, North Melbourne — these clubs need to either improve their development infrastructure, supplement via the trade table, or accept that their list ceiling is constrained.

**The 3-to-1 spread from top to bottom:** Western Bulldogs at 15.6% convert elite picks at nearly three times the rate of St Kilda at 5.7%. That spread reflects development infrastructure, retention capability, and cultural stability — not luck. **[data]**

**The two trade models that work alongside drafting:**
1. **Geelong model:** Average drafting + late extraction + elite trade imports (Dangerfield, Cameron) + retention = 4 flags. Works when the core is deep enough to justify premium trade prices.
2. **GWS/Melbourne model:** High-pick access (expansion club or sustained losing) + infrastructure that converts picks + retention = contender. Works when the list base is deep enough to hold against exits.
