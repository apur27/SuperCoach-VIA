# AFL 2026 team list analysis - all 18 clubs

> [← Back to Coaches Strategy Corner](README.md) | [← Back to main README](../../README.md)

This is a detailed list-construction read of every AFL club heading into mid-2026. Each section is built around nine load-bearing questions: list identity and recruiting philosophy, the cornerstone tier, the contributor tier, the development pipeline, position-by-position depth, the structural vulnerabilities, the tactical identity the personnel forces on the coach, the draft-pick trajectory ahead, and the cycle-stage outlook for 2026. Stat anchors (disposal volume, contested possessions, scoring, identity tags) are pulled from `docs/afl-team-analysis-2026.md`. Draft-pedigree details rely on football knowledge - where specific pick numbers are uncertain, the language is hedged ("approx. pick N", "early second round").

This is not a stats document. It is the list-strategy lens applied across the league - what each club's list is built to do, and what it cannot do without paying a structural price.

---

## How to read each section

- **List identity** is the philosophy in a paragraph: draft-vs-trade ratio, when the core was recruited, demographic shape (young / peak / ageing), and recruiting philosophy (academy-first / best-available / position-targeted).
- **Tier 1 - the cornerstone** names six to eight players whose absence would force a structural re-write of the team. Pick / acquisition, year recruited, position, role, contract status.
- **Tier 2 - the depth that separates contenders from pretenders** names four to six players who are not cornerstones but whose presence determines whether the side is finals-credible. List depth lives here.
- **Pipeline - next gen** names two to three rookie / academy / next-gen players who could step into the senior side inside 1-2 seasons, with what they add.
- **Position depth** rates each line 1-5 (1 = crisis, 3 = adequate, 5 = elite).
- **List vulnerabilities** is the honest read on where the list is one injury from crisis, with tactical consequence and fixability per item.
- **Tactical identity explained by list** is the connection between roster shape and match-day style, plus the structural matchups that hurt and help the side.
- **Draft pick trajectory** is the multi-year list lens: what picks does the club hold, where will those picks land, and what should they target.
- **2026 outlook** is the cycle-stage read with one trajectory-changing event flagged and a realistic finish range.

## The 18 clubs

### Adelaide Crows

**List identity:** A draft-built list rebuilt through high picks across the 2017-2021 window, now tilting from "developing" to "improving." The core was assembled across multiple top-ten draft selections (Thilthorpe, Keays, Murray, Berry) with selective trade-table top-ups (Dawson from Sydney, Rankine from Gold Coast, Pedlar via mature-age recruitment). Demographically the list is young and peaking - the spine sits in the 23-27 bracket. The recruiting philosophy is SANFL-pipeline-first (the club has a structural advantage with local-talent retention) supplemented by surgical trade-table moves for outside class and leadership. The bias is toward two-way midfielders and pace at ball-carrying positions.

**List vulnerabilities:**
1. **Second key forward beside Thilthorpe is unsettled.** If Thilthorpe goes down the forward 50 collapses to a small-and-medium structure with no contested-mark anchor, and the side goes from "kick it long to a target" to "force a contest and crumb." Fixable this trade period (mature-age tall, FA pickup), but the problem has persisted across two windows.
2. **Ruck depth behind O'Brien is one injury from crisis.** Adelaide are 18th for hit-outs at 8.8/g - the position is structurally weak even when O'Brien is fit, and a 2-3 game absence forces a part-time tall (Thilthorpe / Borlase) to ruck and removes a forward target. Multi-year fix; the draft pipeline does not currently hold a settled successor.
3. **Outside speed in transition is mid-tier.** The half-back unit moves the ball, but the wing-runner profile that top-eight clubs carry to break lines is thinner here. A contested-ball-heavy gameplan papers over it; against quick transition sides (Sydney, Gold Coast) the gap shows. Fixable through draft over 2-3 years.
4. **Inside-midfield ball-winning behind Dawson/Crouch is mid-tier.** The contested possessions ranking (7th in 2026) reads better than the personnel; a Berry/Soligo step-up is what holds the line.

**Tactical identity explained by list:** The list forces a defensive, rebound-and-counter identity. Adelaide are 2nd for rebound-50s (41.8/g) and 4th for tackles (59.5/g) but 16th for disposals - they don't dominate possession, they absorb and counter. The personnel does not allow a kick-mark slow-tempo game (no second tall, no elite outside class on the wing) and does not allow a clearance-first contested game (mid-tier inside-mid ball-winning, weak ruck). What it does allow is sit-deep, force opposition turnovers, run the ball through Milera/Hinge, and feed Thilthorpe / Rankine. Bad structural matchup: Brisbane (their kick-mark game neutralises Adelaide's pressure tools and their clearance dominance starves the rebound). Good structural matchup: Essendon (mirror-image possession-light side, but Adelaide's pressure and rebound is decisively higher).

**Draft pick trajectory:** Mid-table drafts likely - Adelaide will not be picking inside the top five with the current trajectory. Expect picks in the 8-15 range across the next two drafts. Target priorities are second key forward, ruck successor, and outside-class wing. If both drafts go well, the list in 2028 has a settled second tall, a ruck succession plan in place, and one more wing-runner - which is enough to push from "improving top-eight" to "open contender."

**2026 outlook:** Late-stage development - the list is one or two trade-period upgrades away from being a settled top-eight side. The single trajectory-changing event would be securing a contested-marking second tall in the 2026 trade period; without it the ceiling is finals-week-one. Realistic 2026 finish: 8th-12th.

### Brisbane Lions

**List identity:** A premiership-window list built on a 50/50 draft-and-trade model: the academy and 2014-2017 draft classes built the spine, the trade table layered on the elite finishers. The core was recruited in two waves - the 2014-2016 academy and homegrown drafts (Andrews, McCluggage, Rich, Robinson) and the 2017-2020 trade-table coups (Neale from Fremantle, Cameron from Adelaide, Lyons from Gold Coast). Demographically the list is squarely in its peak window with an ageing midfield core (Neale, Lyons) and a younger second wave (Ashcroft, Dunkley, Fletcher). Recruiting philosophy is best-available with a strong contested-ball bias - they trade for ball-winners, they draft for tall stocks, and they protect the academy as a structural advantage.

**List vulnerabilities:**
1. **Midfield core ageing - Neale, Lyons, Dunkley all on the back nine.** If the next-tier midfielder (Ashcroft, Levi Ashcroft) doesn't step into a 30-disposal contested-ball role inside 12-24 months, the contested-ball edge that defines the side narrows. Multi-year list problem rather than a trade-period fix - succession is already in motion via the academy.
2. **Tackle pressure ranks 18th (46.4/g).** The list is built for ball use, not pressure - against high-pressure opposition (Sydney, Fremantle), Brisbane lose the pressure war and rely on winning the kick-mark battle to compensate. Not fixable; it is a list-design choice.
3. **Forward-half pressure outside Cameron/Rayner is mid-tier.** With Allen as a contested marker rather than a pressure runner, the small-forward pressure unit needs Cameron and Rayner to carry the workload. A short-term injury here costs the side scoreboard pressure off turnover.
4. **Ageing kick-tank - the kick-first identity (1st for kicks, 1st for marks) depends on McCluggage, Rich, Zorko remaining elite.** If two of three regress in the same season, the system creaks. Multi-year recruiting implication.

**Tactical identity explained by list:** List shape forces a kick-first, mark-heavy, slow-tempo game - 1st for kicks (252.5/g), 1st for marks (122.8/g), 1st for clearances (41.0/g). With elite contested midfielders, the league's best key-back pair, McCluggage/Ashcroft as outside class, and Allen now in as a third tall, Brisbane can dictate tempo by foot and finish chains with three marking targets inside 50. The list does not allow a high-pressure chase-and-tackle game (18th for tackles is a list signature, not a coaching choice). Good structural matchup: Essendon, Adelaide, North Melbourne (Brisbane's kick-mark game suffocates possession-light sides). Bad structural matchup: Sydney (the only team that can match Brisbane's contested-ball volume while applying significantly more pressure - Sydney's forward-half pressure is the single tactical problem Brisbane have not solved).

**Draft pick trajectory:** Lower-end picks across the next two drafts (15-25 range, plus academy access). Brisbane will not have top-five draft access while the window is open. Targets: small/medium-forward pressure depth, midfield succession (academy already producing), tall-back depth behind Andrews. If both drafts deliver, the list in 2028 has Levi Ashcroft as a 25+ disposal mid, a settled small-forward replacement for Cameron, and continued tall-back depth - the window stays open three more seasons.

**2026 outlook:** Open premiership window with one to two seasons before the midfield core needs reinforcing. The single trajectory-changing event would be a Neale season-ending injury (the contested-ball volume is irreplaceable). Realistic 2026 finish: 1st-4th, premiership contender.

### Carlton

The core was recruited 2013-2018 (Cripps, Walsh, McKay, Weitering) with trade-period reinforcements stacked on top. Demographically the list is in its mid-window peak (24-28) with a thinning second tier and uneven depth across the bottom half. Recruiting philosophy is best-available top-end via the draft, then targeted trades for outside class - the bottom of the list has been treated as a residual rather than as a core build asset, which is why the gap between top-six and bottom-twelve is wider than most contenders carry.

If both miss, the gap between top-six and depth widens fatally.

**2026 outlook:** Mid-window with a rebalancing task. The trade period of late 2026 will define the next two seasons - the ruck/forward call is the single trajectory-changing piece. Realistic 2026 finish: 7th-12th. Window stays cracked open but is not currently in elite-contender shape.

### Collingwood

**List identity:** A father-son-and-trade list with a single generational midfielder (Nick Daicos) carrying disproportionate load. The core was recruited in two waves: the 2014-2018 premiership-era homegrown and trade additions (Pendlebury legacy, Sidebottom, De Goey, Moore father-son, Howe), and the more recent reinforcement of the spine (Nick Daicos father-son 2021, Schultz trade, Houston trade from Port 2025, Perryman trade from GWS). The 2024-2025 trade outflow (Mihocek to Melbourne, Mason Cox to Fremantle) has thinned the tall stocks. Demographically the list is the oldest in the competition and known for it - a deliberate veteran-retention model designed to extend a window. Recruiting philosophy is father-son-first where available, then trade for premiership-tier role players, with the draft used sparingly. The list has prioritised retention over regeneration for several seasons.

**List vulnerabilities:**
1. **Age across the premiership core.** Pendlebury, Sidebottom, Howe are all past peak. The list is carrying a cohort that needs to be replaced inside two drafts or the window slams shut - and the draft-pick capital to do it is thinner than it should be after a decade of trade-period top-ups. Multi-year list-build problem - not fixable inside one trade window.
2. **Ruck position is a chronic structural weakness.** 15th for hit-outs in 2026; the list has not solved the position since trading in Brodie Grundy and then trading him out. A 2-game absence at any stage forces a part-time tall to ruck. Trade priority.
3. **Forward depth beyond the small-forward unit is thin.** With Mihocek now at Melbourne and De Goey injury-prone, the marking tall load falls on McStay and any cameo forward roles - an injury to either thins the inside-50 to "Daicos kicks short to mediums."
4. **Inside-mid load on Nick Daicos.** The personnel asks Daicos to lead the league in disposals (36.6/g - league-leading) because the second-tier inside-mid options (De Goey when on) cannot carry the same load. A Daicos injury collapses the entire ball-movement system.

With Nick Daicos as the spread-and-rebound anchor and McStay / Elliott / Hill as the forward unit, Collingwood are built to control the ball outside the contest, slow the game, and score from chains rather than from clearance. The personnel does not allow a contested-ball game - 18th for clearances is not a coaching choice, it is what the ruck/inside-mid mix produces. Bad structural matchup: Brisbane, Fremantle, Sydney (any side that wins centre bounces repeatedly forces Collingwood to play a game they are not built for). Good structural matchup: Essendon, Carlton (sides whose forward-line architecture also struggles, against whom Collingwood's possession control wins by attrition).

**Draft pick trajectory:** Lower picks while the window is open (15-25 range), with a likely fall-off when retirements hit. Trade capital is the constraint. Targets: ruck (priority), inside-mid succession behind Daicos, key-forward depth. If both drafts deliver and the trade table is used aggressively for younger reinforcements, the list in 2028 has a soft regeneration around Daicos and Moore as the new core. If they don't, 2028-2029 is a full rebuild.

**2026 outlook:** Closing premiership window - the next 18 months are the last clean tilt with this core before regeneration becomes mandatory. The trajectory-changing event is whether De Goey can deliver one more elite season at peak fitness; without it the side's forward-half scoring drops a tier. Realistic 2026 finish: 4th-9th.

## Caveats and what this document is not

- **Draft pick numbers are best-effort from football knowledge.** Where the specific pick number is uncertain, the document hedges ("approximately pick N", "mid-round find", "early first round"). The repo's CSV data does not carry draft-pedigree fields, so this is the limit of what can be verified here.
- **Player-club allocations are verified against `afltables.com/afl/stats/2026.html` as of mid-2026.** A player not appearing in a club's 2026 stats table either means they have left that club or they have not yet played a game in 2026 (injury, suspension, omission); the doc treats afltables as authoritative for "playing for this club in 2026" but does not assume absence equals departure.
- **Position depth ratings are subjective scaled assessments.** They are calibrated against the league rather than against an absolute standard - a 5/5 ruck at one club is "league elite" not "perfect in all conditions."
- **No injury-list overlay.** Players currently injured or unavailable are not flagged - the list-construction read assumes all named players are fit.
- **The 2026 stat anchors are season-to-date and small-sample.** The leading-disposal and identity tags come from `docs/afl-team-analysis-2026.md` after 8-9 rounds played; they are honest but they are not yet settled hierarchies.
- **Contract status notes are general rather than precise.** Specific end-of-deal years are not tracked at this level - "re-signed long-term" means a multi-year deal, "veteran" means late-career, etc.
- **Pipeline players are illustrative, not exhaustive.** Each club has 5-15 development-list players; the doc names 2-3 most likely to break through.
- **This is a list lens, not a tactical brief.** Match-day adjustments, opposition-specific match-ups, and weekly game-planning sit in the per-match docs in `docs/coaches-strategy-corner/`, not here.
- **No prediction of premierships, individual awards, or specific player futures.** The document frames where each list sits in its cycle; it does not call who lifts the cup.
