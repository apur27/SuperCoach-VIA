## FootyStrategy tactical read

[Tier: Probationary] [Horizon: weekly + multi-year] [Lenses: 6 activated]

This is the council's tactical and coaching interpretation of Round 9 2026, Adelaide 14.14.98 def Richmond 9.7.61 by 37 points at the M.C.G. The Scientist agent has owned the data layer (final-snapshot stats, prediction audit, AF quarter splits, conversion arithmetic). This document does not re-state those numbers - it translates them into football language and surfaces the coaching choices behind the scoreboard. Activated lenses on the question: the Structuralist, the Match-up Tactician, the Tempo Architect, the Conditioner, the Talent Developer, and the List Strategist. The Innovator and Culture Custodian sit out - this game did not turn on a novel structural exploit, and Richmond's standards were not the failure point.

### The tactical narrative

The game lived at three levels: a midfield contest decided by ruck supply and the half-time positional reset, a forward-50 phase that exposed Richmond's entry-quality ceiling against the league's second-best rebound system, and a defensive structure that gave Adelaide's half-back distributors the running room to score from chain.

**Midfield contest - centre clearances and the structural unlock.** Adelaide entered as the 18th-ranked hit-out side. With Samson Ryan scratched, Lachlan McAndrew solo-rucked 33 of Adelaide's 37 hit-outs and Adelaide won the hit-out count by 18. That single number flips the entire centre-bounce dynamic the pre-game brief had built around. Once the ruck constraint was lifted, Adelaide's clearance work was no longer "constrained by their ruck" (their season rank of 16/18 for clearances had been a ruck-constrained number) - they were free to win first use and run. The Structuralist read on this: Richmond's midfield was not beaten on personnel - Ross out-tackled and out-worked Berry decisively (Ross 96 AF, 107 SC, 6 tackles; Berry 69 AF, 3 tackles). The midfield was beaten on **supply chain.** When the ruck-tap-to-clearance pipeline reverses on you and the opposition's tackle pressure spikes to 161% of season rate, the contested ball gets won at the source - inside the centre square - and what looks like a midfield disposal contest is actually a stoppage-supply contest you have already lost by the time the ball lands at a midfielder's feet.

The Tempo Architect's read: Adelaide controlled when this game accelerated. Q1 and Q2 ran at a tempo that suited Richmond's defensive set (slower, more contested, kick-mark phases through Short and Vlastuin). Q3 ran at the tempo that suited Adelaide - sharp transition off the rebound, two-and-three handball receivers ahead of the ball, kicks to running half-forwards. Tempo control sat with the side that won first use, and first use was decided at the ruck contest.

**Forward-50 entries - the conversion crisis is a delivery crisis.** Richmond kicked 9.7 from 16 scoring shots (56% accuracy - better than Adelaide's 50%). The shot accuracy was acceptable; the shot **volume** was not. Adelaide had 28 scoring shots; Richmond had 16. The headline "9 behinds = conversion crisis" mis-locates the problem. The behinds were spread across the day and reflect honest set-shot variance against a deep MCG wing. The real failure was that Richmond could not generate inside-50 entries at a rate that gave their conversion percentage scoreboard meaning. Eighteenth for kicks in 2026, 18th for marks, 18th for goals - this is a side whose ball-use ceiling does not produce 25 inside-50s a game against top-eight defences, and against the league's second-best rebound system (Adelaide 41.8 rebound-50s/g), every entry Richmond bombed long came back.

The Structuralist on entry quality: long kicks to space without a marking-tall target are concession passes against this Adelaide defence. Worrell intercepts at 6.4 marks/g and Milera distributes from there - the rebound chain is the third-most reliable scoring source on the Adelaide list. Tom Lynch finished with 14 disposals and 1 confirmed goal. He was not supplied. The Talent Developer's read: Lynch can still mark a contest at peak (3 marks, the late Q4 goal off a Q4 7:11 contested mark proves it), but a 33-year-old key forward who does not have a settled second tall to share the contest with cannot manufacture his own supply. He is a finisher of the chain; he is not the chain. Richmond's list does not currently hold the player who is the chain.

**Defensive structure and the Laird-Milera-Worrell rebound axis.** Adelaide's half-back unit ran the game's transition engine. Laird (27 disposals, 12 marks, 81% disposal efficiency, 105 SC) operated as the link from defence to midfield - not a runner who scored, a connector who chained possessions from the back six to the forward 50 cleanly. Milera at 34 disposals and 94% DE was the cleanest user on the ground. Worrell at 92% TOG was the structural anchor - the intercept-and-launch player whose presence allowed Laird and Milera to take the ball off the back of his marks. The Match-up Tactician's read: Richmond never set a forward press capable of pinning these three behind the ball. The forward line did not have the pressure unit - small forwards are a list-construction gap on this Richmond roster (Daniel Rioli to Gold Coast, Shai Bolton to Fremantle, the post-2017-2020 pressure identity gone) - so Laird and Milera ran into space every rebound chain.

### Three moments that decided the game

**Moment 1 - the team-list announcement (Friday).** Ryan was scratched. The brief had identified hit-outs as "the single biggest structural lever in the game" and built the centre-bounce plan around 16.3 hit-outs/g of Ryan dominance. When Ryan came out, no Plan B was substituted in. The Conditioner and Structuralist both flag this: the team's stoppage-supply plan should never be a single-player conditional. If your win condition depends on one ruck being fit and available, you have a list-depth problem dressed as a tactical plan. The match was tactically losable before the bounce.

**Moment 2 - the half-time reset (~3:00pm).** Adelaide moved Jordan Dawson from half-back to midfield, Nick Murray from key forward back to defence. This was not a tempo escalation - it was a structural correction of a Walker-cover compromise that wasn't working (Murray as KPF "was just not working" per commentary, Adelaide -24 AF at the long break). What Richmond's matchup zone had been set against (a half-back Dawson, a key-forward Murray) ceased to exist in the 90 seconds of dressing-room time. The Match-up Tactician's read: this is the single most important coaching moment of the game. Every Richmond matchup involving Dawson, Murray, Worrell, or Sholl needed a "contingency branch" for positional flex. Adelaide's coaching staff have used this exact reshuffle pattern across 2025-26; it should have been on Richmond's half-time card with a named insertion ("if Dawson moves into the midfield, we add Sam Banks to the centre square as the fourth on-baller and pull Hopper to a wing"). It was not.

**Moment 3 - Q3 0:53, Rachele snaps after Rankine strips Balta.** The visible kickoff of Adelaide's Q3 burst. Within 4 minutes 38 seconds of the bounce, Adelaide led 71-19 AF in the quarter. Four players over 40 Q3 AF (Dawson 48, Milera 45, Cook 42, Rankine 41) against Richmond's best Q3 contribution of 32 AF (Trainor, a late inclusion). The Conditioner's read: this is where the conditioning gap shows. Two quarters of absorbing tackle pressure at the structural ceiling (Adelaide tackling at 161% of season rate) compresses Richmond's energy reserves, and the Q3 reset arrives at exactly the worst time. The Tempo Architect's read: Adelaide held high-impact forwards in reserve through Q1-Q2 (suppressed inside a compromised structure) and unleashed them in Q3 against an opposition matchup-tracking system fatigued from 90 minutes of in-game work. The Q3 burst was structural - Adelaide's natural ceiling, finally allowed to operate - not random variance.

### Coaching diagnosis: what went wrong with Richmond

**1. Were Richmond's structure choices correct?** Partially. The pre-game brief had three load-bearing structural calls: (a) Vlastuin on Walker (voided when Walker DNP); (b) Ross on Berry (executed and won - Ross out-played Berry on every dimension); (c) Ryan as the structural lever in the ruck contest (voided when Ryan DNP). Two of the three structural calls were void on team-list announcement. The Structuralist's verdict: the structures Richmond chose for the players they had were defensible. The structures they chose for the players they were missing were not - the brief had no documented contingency branches for Walker-out or Ryan-out, and the on-day adjustments were reactive rather than rehearsed. **The execution of structure on the day was a 6/10; the contingency planning was a 3/10.**

**2. Was the forward setup wrong for this opposition?** Yes - and the brief partially flagged it but couldn't fix it. Lone-tall Lynch against an Adelaide defence that doesn't rely on a dominant second key back (Worrell is an intercept-and-launch player, not a stopper; Borlase, Michalanney and Curtin rotate the second tall role) was the wrong setup for two reasons. First, Lynch as the only contested-mark target made him the entire opposition defensive focus - Adelaide could double-team him without exposing a second matchup. Second, Adelaide's defence is at its best against territory entries (rebound-50s rank 2/18); a lone tall who needs entries-to-advantage cannot get them against a side built to punish long kicks. The Talent Developer's read: the better setup with this list is a two-medium-tall, three-small structure - Balta forward, Lynch deeper or rotating high, Faull as a third option, Campbell and Maurice Rioli Jr as the small-forward pressure. The 2026 version of Richmond's forward line is not a "lone tall" forward line; it is a "we don't have two talls, so play to your smalls" forward line. The structure on the day was hoping Lynch could be what the 2018-2020 list let him be, and the list cannot support that any more.

**3. Did Taranto / Hopper / Lalor lose the midfield battle?** They did not - the midfield contest, judged on disposals and personal numbers, was competitive. Taranto 28 disposals, Hopper 22, Lalor (no public number in the cited tables but the brief's seasonal 16.6 d/g profile would place him at around 18-20 on the day) - this is a midfield that is touching the ball. **The problem is not whether they won the contest; the problem is what their disposals produced.** Taranto's 28 disposals came with 18 clangers in the cell (per the Scientist's audit) and a 64% disposal efficiency - the highest-disposal-getter who turned the ball over most. The Match-up Tactician on Taranto specifically: the brief flagged a -2.91 disposals/round downward trend pre-match. He is a player whose role should be managed, not extended. Asking him to be the contested-ball ceiling and the clearance leader and the rebound link is asking for the clanger floor he posted. Hopper at CV 0.14 was reliable (22 every week is what the data said and 22 is what he got). Lalor remains pre-cornerstone - he is the generational hope, but he is not yet the player who closes a 25-minute Q3 burst. The Talent Developer's read: this midfield is *competitive*; it is not yet *decisive*. There is a difference, and the difference is what loses a game by 37.

**4. Was the Rankine-on-Vlastuin match-up the correct call?** No. Rankine finished 33 disposals, 158 AF, 147 SC, 10 marks, 8 tackles - the dominant player on the ground. The brief had identified Rankine as a "must-contain, mobile forward, match with running defender." Vlastuin is a key-back lockdown body, not a running defender - the body that was supposed to be on Walker. When Walker DNP and the matchup card had to be re-shuffled, Vlastuin should have gone to Thilthorpe and the Rankine assignment should have gone to a running half-back (Tom Brown or Sam Banks playing as a defensive winger). The Match-up Tactician's verdict on the call: the Rankine assignment as actually played was not the brief's plan - it was the brief's plan corrupted by the late Walker scratch, and no one re-issued the matchup card. **The fix is not "different player on Rankine"; the fix is "the matchup card is re-issued at the team-list announcement, not at the first bounce".**

**5. Is this a coaching problem or a list problem?** Both. The Scientist's structural autopsy and the council's lens reads converge on the same answer: the list construction sets the ceiling, the coaching sets the floor, and on Saturday Richmond hit neither the ceiling they could have nor the floor the coaching staff should have demanded. The List Strategist's read: this Richmond list is 18th for disposals, kicks, marks, and goals - those rankings are list-construction facts, not coaching choices. No tactical adjustment converts an 18th-ranked marking side into a top-eight marking side inside one trade period. Lynch past peak, no settled second tall, the small-forward pressure unit gone (Rioli to Gold Coast, Bolton to Fremantle) - **the personnel does not allow a top-eight scoreboard against a top-eight defence.** The coaching problem on the day was three failures: (a) no Ryan-out / Walker-out contingency planning, (b) no half-time positional-flex contingency for Dawson, (c) a forward setup that asked Lynch to be the 2018 version of himself with a 2026 list around him. **The list problem is bigger and longer-term: this side cannot generate the inside-50 volume to convert its decent accuracy into scoreboard pressure, and that is two drafts away from being fixed.** The honest verdict: 30% coaching, 70% list. The coaching component is fixable inside seven days; the list component is fixable inside three trade periods.

### Adelaide's formula

Adelaide's win was a system win, not a talent win - and the council's reads converge on what the system actually was.

**The press-and-transition pattern.** Adelaide's "blitzkrieg" framing from the live read is a useful descriptor for the Q3 burst, but the underlying mechanic is more durable than a one-quarter surge. The pattern: Rankine and Keays press at half-forward to slow the opposition's defensive 50 exit; Berry (when fit) and Dawson tackle in the corridor to force handball clangers; Milera, Laird and Worrell intercept the panicked kick out of defence; the rebound chain runs through three handballs and a running kick to a leading Rankine or Rachele inside the forward arc. Rankine's 8 tackles on the day is the elite signature - this is a forward who completes the press, not a forward who waits for supply. The Conditioner's read: this press pattern is conditioning-dependent and Adelaide's list is fit enough to run it for stretches but not for four quarters - the Q1-Q2 sag (RI +27 in Q2 AF) is the cost of holding it back; the Q3 burst is the return on saving it. The Tempo Architect's read: the press is the tempo control device. When Adelaide want to accelerate, the press fires; when they want to manage clock, the press relaxes (Q4 Dawson 5 AF after a 48 Q3, the visible signature of throttling down).

**Dawson as a half-back mid - the midfield overload mechanic.** The structural insight that Richmond should study: Adelaide can credibly play **four genuine on-ball midfielders** at any one centre bounce because Dawson, Milera, and Cook can all flex into the contest from a starting position outside it. Pre-half-time, Adelaide ran three (Berry, Cook, and whoever was rotating). Post-half-time they ran four (Dawson, Berry, Cook, Rankine running mid-forward). The Structuralist's read: this is the midfield overload that broke Richmond's matchup zone. Richmond set Hopper, Taranto, Ross, Prestia on three Adelaide mids plus one tag/wing - a 3v3 plus a free body. When Adelaide turned a 3v3 into a 4v3, Richmond did not have the personnel rotation to add a fourth body without exposing somewhere else. The fix - and the broader principle - is that against any list that holds three or more positional-flex midfielders (Adelaide, Brisbane, Hawthorn, GWS), the matchup card must include a "fourth-on-baller insertion" plan with a named player held back from a wing or HBF role.

**Is the "blitzkrieg" framing accurate?** Half-true. Rankine's 8 tackles is genuinely elite and represents Adelaide's modern identity - forward pressure that is unusual for a key-forward-adjacent role. But the framing risks over-personalising what was a *systemic* output. Take Berry out (3 tackles vs season 8.0) and Adelaide's team tackle count went **up**, not down. The blitzkrieg is a property of the structure, not the player. The Culture Custodian, sitting back in on this point: Adelaide's pressure identity is what they contest. That is how it survives one player having a flat day. Richmond's pressure identity in 2026 is contested-ball-by-default rather than chosen-pressure - which is a list-construction fact, not a values gap, and the answer is in the trade period, not the Tuesday review.

### 2026 season diagnosis

**Is the 37-point loss to Adelaide typical of what Richmond's list will produce against top-eight sides all season?** Yes, with one important qualification.

The List Strategist's read is the load-bearing one: Richmond are 1-7 with a -43.5 average margin through eight games. The Adelaide loss at -37 is *better than the season average* against any opposition, and Adelaide are not the toughest top-eight side on the fixture. Against Sydney, Brisbane, Hawthorn, or Gold Coast - all sides whose stat profile mismatches Richmond's at the same structural extreme that Adelaide's did - the expected margin is wider, not narrower. The 18th-for-disposals, 18th-for-kicks, 18th-for-marks, 18th-for-goals profile is what produces -43.5 average margins; the structural matchup against top-eight defences will not improve over the rest of 2026 because the list will not improve over the rest of 2026.

**The qualification:** the Adelaide loss specifically contained two coaching-fixable failures (the Ryan-out contingency gap and the Dawson half-time reshuffle blind spot) that turned a likely -25 game into a -37 game. A version of this game with rehearsed contingencies and a re-issued matchup card at the team-list announcement is a 4-goal loss, not a 6-goal loss. **That is the coaching dividend available to Richmond inside seven days.** Against the rest of the 2026 fixture, the dividend is the same shape: tighter contingency planning, better mid-match positional re-reads, more disciplined forward-line role allocation - none of which fix the list, all of which prevent the list from producing margins worse than what the list deserves.

**Concrete verdict:** Richmond's 2026 season floor is 16th-18th and the Adelaide game is within one standard deviation of the expected margin against a top-eight side. The list is the binding constraint; the coaching is the variance. Tighter coaching narrows the variance downward (fewer 50-point losses); it does not lift the floor. The fix to lift the floor is the next two top-five picks (forward / key back / ruck succession), and that is the List Strategist's territory, not the Tuesday review's.

### Five prescriptions for Round 10

These are tactical, list-construction-respecting, and grounded in what this Richmond list can actually do next Saturday. The Innovator and Talent Developer co-sign each; the Conditioner and Match-up Tactician have flagged tripwires.

**1. Re-issue the matchup card at team-list announcement, not at the first bounce - and tag every assignment as "first-half / all-game / with-contingency".** The Ryan-out, Walker-out, Dawson-reshuffle gaps all sit on the same root: matchup planning treated as a pre-game artefact rather than a live document. The fix: at every team-list announcement (Thursday) and at every quarter-time and half-time break, the matchup card is re-issued with every assignment re-tagged. Dawson-equivalents (positional flex midfielders) get explicit "with-contingency" tags naming the alternative defender. **Tripwire:** if the Round 10 opponent runs a positional reshuffle in the first three quarters and Richmond's matchup tracking does not adjust within five minutes, the protocol failed and the next iteration needs a sharper trigger.

**2. Play the forward line the list allows, not the forward line the 2018-2020 list allowed.** Lynch as the lone contested-marking tall is a structure for a list that does not exist any more. Round 10 setup: two-medium-tall (Lynch and Balta forward, with Faull as a third option), three small-forward pressure unit (Campbell, Maurice Rioli Jr, and a fourth pressure piece from the wing rotation), no long kicks to space, every inside-50 to a contest the smalls can crumb. **Tripwire:** if the side generates fewer than 20 inside-50s in a half against the Round 10 opposition, the structure is wrong for the personnel and the next iteration is "accept the territory loss, play kick-mark through Short and Vlastuin, score from rebound only".

**3. Build a "Ryan-out forward supply" play-script and rehearse it on Tuesday.** Three named alternatives - (a) Taranto as a marking-midfielder kick-mark chain operator, (b) Lynch dropping deeper as a half-forward crumbing under Trainor's marks, (c) explicit accept-defeat-on-territory and play the kick-out + transition game from Short. The play-script picks one for the Round 10 opponent and runs it for the full game; the in-game live read at the five-minute mark of Q1 confirms it. The Structuralist's load-bearing point: a contingency plan that has not been rehearsed is not a contingency plan. **Tripwire:** if Ryan plays Round 10, the script goes in the back pocket. If Ryan is out and any of (a)/(b)/(c) is not visibly executed by the first quarter break, the script wasn't rehearsed.

**4. Q3 protocol: the matchup card gets re-issued at half-time regardless of score.** The Adelaide reset was not the first time a top-eight side has reshuffled at the long break, and it will not be the last. The protocol: at every half-time, the senior assistant re-reads the opposition's structure and re-issues the matchup card if any of Adelaide / Brisbane / Hawthorn / GWS / Sydney style positional flexes are detected. The Conditioner's load-bearing add: this protocol is also a conditioning load lever - the Q3 capitulation in this game (-305 AF) was partly fatigue-on-stale-matchups, not pure conditioning gap. Refreshed assignments distribute the cognitive load. **Tripwire:** if Richmond's Q3 AF in Round 10 falls more than 100 points below their Q1+Q2 average, the protocol either wasn't run or wasn't sharp enough.

**5. Manage Taranto's role: contested ball or rebound link, not both.** The brief flagged Taranto's -2.91 disposals/round trend pre-game and the on-day output (28 disposals, 18 clangers, 64% DE) confirmed the prediction. Asking him to be the contested-ball ceiling AND the clearance leader AND the rebound link is asking for the turnover floor he produced. The Talent Developer's prescription: pick one role per game. Round 10 version: Taranto as the contested-ball specialist at centre bounces only, with Ross taking the rebound-link role and Hopper holding the wing. Reduce Taranto's total disposal load by 4-6 per game; the clanger count drops faster than the disposal count, and the side's net output improves. **Tripwire:** if Taranto's clangers in Round 10 are above 10 with disposal efficiency below 70%, the role-narrowing didn't take and the next iteration is a tagger-style "shadow Taranto on the opposition's #1 mid" defensive role instead.
