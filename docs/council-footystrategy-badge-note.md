## Part 1 — README badge framing

[Tier: Probationary] [Horizon: in-season] [Lenses: 3 activated — Structuralist, Culture Custodian, Innovator]

**The situation.** The README data badge currently reads `data-2026 season round 12-green` — the last *completed* round of match data sitting in the cabinet. The published predictions page (`docs/afl-predictions-2026.md`) leads with disposal leaders for *Round 13* — the round being predicted *toward*. So the badge and the headline product point at two different round numbers, and a reader meeting this repo for the first time has to reconcile them without help.

**What a first-time reader interprets.** A reader who sees "round 12" reads it as *currency* — "how fresh is this thing?" — and concludes the data is complete through Round 12, which is exactly true. But the same reader, one click later, lands on a page selling Round 13 tips and feels a half-beat of friction: did I miss a refresh? Is this stale? The badge undersold the product. Conversely, a reader who sees "round 13" reads it as *the offer* — "this tells me about Round 13" — which is what they actually came for, and the predictions page then confirms the promise instead of contradicting it. The risk on the "round 13" side is the opposite failure: a reader could infer that Round 13 has been *played and ingested*, when in fact Round 13 is the unplayed target. That is the more dangerous misread, because it overstates the data state rather than understating the product, and this repo's entire identity is honest data state.

**Recommendation — label the data state, not the prediction target, but label it as state explicitly.** Keep the badge anchored to the completed round, but remove the ambiguity that lets a reader mistake it for the prediction round. The cleanest fix is to make the badge say what kind of number it is: `data through R12 · predicting R13`, or two separate badges — one green "data: 2026 R1–12 complete", one blue "predicting: R13". This is the Culture Custodian's call carrying the most weight: the repo's moat is that every number is honest about what it is, and a badge that could be read as "Round 13 is in the cabinet" violates that standard for a marketing half-yard that isn't worth it. The Structuralist agrees on structural grounds — the badge is a *defensive* element (it states what we have), the predictions headline is the *attacking* element (it states what we offer), and the two should not be collapsed into one number that does both jobs badly. The Innovator's dissent is noted and minor: a single forward-facing "R13" badge reads more like a live product and less like an archive, which has audience appeal — but it loses the veto when the gain is presentation and the cost is a truthfulness ambiguity. Hence Probationary, not Settled: the call is clear on principle, but the exact rendering (combined badge vs. two badges) is a presentation choice the maintainer should pick.

**Tripwire.** If readers start opening issues or asking "is the Round 13 data in yet?" — i.e., the badge is being read as *completed* Round 13 — the dual-number framing has failed and the badge should collapse back to a single unambiguous "data through R12" with the prediction target moved entirely into the predictions page header. Equally, if analytics show readers bouncing off the README *before* reaching the predictions page, the badge is underselling the offer and the forward-facing R13 label earns a second look.

**Caveat propagation.** No Scientist finding underwrites this — it is a framing judgement reasoned from the repo's stated honest-data-state value, not from measured reader behaviour. Treat the recommendation as a default to be overridden the moment real audience signal (issues, analytics) contradicts it.

**Out of scope.** The exact shields.io string, colour, and whether to ship one badge or two is a presentation/maintainer decision, not a strategic one.

## Part 2 — Tactical lens on the governance gaps

[Tier: Settled] [Horizon: in-season → multi-year] [Lenses: 2 activated — Structuralist, Culture Custodian]

**What the gaps represent structurally.** Read the council pipeline (BriefBuilder → Scientist → FootyStrategy → DataSentinel → Skeptic) as a team's defensive structure, and the two open gaps stop being plumbing and become holes in the back six. **Gap 1 — pre-commit hook portability** is the difference between a defensive press that only works at home. DataSentinel is the last defender at the door of every commit, the player who refuses to let an unverified stat past the 50. But a pre-commit hook that fires on one machine and silently no-ops on another isn't a defender — it's a defender who only turns up to home games. The structure *looks* sound on the whiteboard, yet the moment the work is done from a different laptop, a contributor's clone, or CI, the press isn't set and a wrong number can walk through unmanned. A defensive structure that depends on which ground you're playing on is not a structure; it's a habit. **Gap 2 — the audit log** is the absence of the post-match review tape. Right now `git log` is the only record of who changed what, which tells you a number changed but not that it was *checked* — you can see the kick, but not whether the defender actually laid the tackle or just waved at it. Without a verification audit trail you can replay, you cannot prove after the fact that the gate did its job; you can only assert it. That is the gap between a back line that says "trust us, we held" and one that can roll the tape and show the contest.

**Why closing them matters for trust in the output.** A team is only as trusted as its worst-defended exit, and an opponent — here, a sceptical reader who catches one unverified stat — only needs to find the structure open *once* to stop believing the whole back six. Closing Gap 1 makes the press travel: the verification gate sets the same way regardless of which machine, contributor, or pipeline runs the commit, so "every number is checked" stops being a claim about the author's discipline and becomes a property of the structure itself — exactly the Culture Custodian's standard that holds when no one is watching. Closing Gap 2 turns assertion into evidence: a replayable audit trail is the tape that lets the repo *show* the gate fired, not just say so, which is what separates a defensible methodology from a marketing line. Together they convert the council from a structure that works when the right people are in the right mood into one that works because the shape forces it to — and a structure that forces its own standard is the only kind that survives turnover, scales past its author, and earns the trust the published output is staking its name on.

**Tripwire.** If a wrong `**[data]**` number reaches a published doc *after* both gaps are closed — via a clone, a CI path, or a commit route the hook doesn't cover — then Gap 1 was closed in name only (the press still has a home-ground dependency) and the portability fix must be re-scoped to cover that route before any further build work proceeds.

**Caveat propagation.** This is a structural-principle read, not a measured one; it assumes the gaps are as described (hook fires inconsistently across environments; no replayable verification trail beyond `git log`). If either assumption is wrong — e.g., the hook is already enforced server-side in CI — the urgency drops accordingly.

**Out of scope.** The implementation of the portable hook and the audit-log schema are Scientist/engineering decisions; this note frames *why* the holes matter, not how to patch them.
