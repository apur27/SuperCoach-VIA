## The one-line version

Essendon is 1-9 through Round 10 of 2026, coming off a 6-17 season, and the man who understands the club's identity better than anyone alive has now been out of the senior coaching system for over a decade — long enough that the football reasons to interview him outweigh the public-relations reasons not to.

This piece argues that James Hird should be on Essendon's shortlist for its next senior coaching appointment. It does not argue that the supplements era should be forgotten, or that his 2014 ASADA finding does not exist. It argues that the football case is real, the time elapsed is long, and the alternative — passing him over reflexively rather than on merit — is a worse process than treating his candidacy seriously.

This is not a one-bad-result narrative. This is a club whose football operation has, on the evidence of the win column, stopped functioning.

The Brad Scott era began at Essendon ahead of the 2023 season **[historical record]** and produced the 2023-2024 plateau visible in the table above. Whether he survives 2026 is the question every Bombers supporter is asking; the question this piece engages is what kind of coach succeeds him — and whether the most thoroughly disqualified candidate of the last decade has, in fact, qualified himself back.

**What kind of coach does this Essendon structurally need?** A 1-9 club is not in the rebuild-mode of a list-strategist appointment. The list has senior pieces; the win column says they are not being organised. The structural diagnosis points toward a coach who:

- Knows the club from the inside well enough that the cultural-rebuild phase compresses
- Has tactical authority earned through football achievement, not through a media résumé
- Can carry the room through a season that will get worse before it gets better
- Can recruit and retain an assistant group on the strength of his name

That profile narrows the field considerably. It includes the obvious sitting-assistant pipeline. It also, if we are honest about what the football case requires, includes James Hird.

### What kind of footballer he was — through the coaching lenses

Hird's playing data, read through the coaching-archetype frames this repo uses elsewhere, sketches three things he understood from the inside:

- **Structuralist read.** He played as a forward-midfielder who connected the lines — 20 disposals and 1.4 goals per game across 253 games is the line of a player whose value was as much in positional structure as in scoreboard impact. A coach who has lived the half-forward/midfield connecting role understands what an inside-50 entry pattern looks like from the receiving end.
- **Tempo Architect read.** 7.3 marks per game in his 1996 Brownlow year **[data]** is, for a half-forward, a marker of a player who set tempo by taking the contested overhead and the leading mark — both deceleration mechanisms in modern coaching language. His own game taught him the value of slowing the ball down at certain times.
- **Talent Developer read.** A 16-year career at one club, captaining from 1998 to 2005 **[historical record — verify against AFL record]**, surrounded by an Essendon dynasty group of Lloyd, Mercuri, Caracella, Wallis, Solomon and Misiti — Hird was, on the public record, the player others raised their game around. The leadership signal is documented in the era.

These are not coaching credentials by themselves. They are the football literacy from which coaching credentials become plausible.

## The coaching case

This is the load-bearing section. It must do real work, not just assert that a great player makes a great coach (the evidence on that proposition is mixed at best). The case for Hird as Essendon's next coach rests on four pillars, in order of weight.

### Pillar 1 — Football IQ, demonstrated and senior

A 253-game career, two premierships, a Brownlow co-medal, three Grand Finals, captaincy of a dynasty group, and a sustained elite Brownlow-vote profile from age 19 to 34 **[data]** is not the résumé of someone who has to learn the senior game on the job. He has lived inside it. The competition has changed since 2007 — the modern stoppage shape, the modern ball-movement template, the modern pressure model are all post-Hird tactics — but the football intelligence that read the 1990s and 2000s game from the inside is portable. What he would need is a modern assistant group; what he would not need is to learn what senior football demands of a player.

### Pillar 2 — Essendon identity, in a club that has lost its outline

This is the pillar that is hardest to argue without sounding sentimental, and the one that, on the football merit, matters most for *this specific job*.

Essendon is currently 1-9 **[data]**. A club that has not won a final since 2004 **[historical record]** and is in its second consecutive bottom-three season is not lacking talent at the margin — it is lacking a defining identity around which the talent organises. The Voss-Carlton entry in this news series argued the same point for Carlton: when the structure is broken, the cultural anchor is the coaching appointment that comes with the deepest knowledge of what the club is supposed to look like.

Hird is one of perhaps three people alive who knows from the inside what Essendon-at-its-best was — the others being Kevin Sheedy and Tim Watson **[historical record]**, both now outside the coaching pipeline. That is a real, scarce, non-substitutable asset.

The counter-form of this argument is important: identity-coach appointments only work when they are paired with modern tactical assistance. The case is not "Hird returns and Essendon plays 2000 football." The case is "Hird supplies the identity around which a modern football department organises." That is a different proposition, and a more defensible one.

### Pillar 3 — The 2011-2013 coaching tenure has been re-evaluated by time

This is the pillar that the public memory most distorts. The actual Essendon record under Hird as senior coach:

| Year | Record | Win % |
|------|--------|-------|
| 2011 | 11W-11L-1D | 50.0% **[data]** |
| 2012 | 11W-11L-0D | 50.0% **[data]** |
| 2013 | 14W-8L-0D | 63.6% **[data]** |

Across his three full coaching seasons, Essendon's record was 36W-30L-1D — a winning record overall and a clearly improving trajectory, with 2013 finishing as the strongest regular-season win-rate of the three **[data]**. The club made finals in 2011 and was finals-eligible on the table at the end of 2013 (the 2013 finals ban was an external administrative outcome, not a football result **[historical record]**). Whatever happened in the football department around supplements, the on-field coaching produced a winning record and an upward arc.

For comparison, the post-Hird Essendon years — once the suspensions had taken effect — produced 6W-16L (2015) and 3W-19L (2016) **[data]**. The collapse coincides with the loss of the playing list to suspensions, not with the change of coach. The football data does not support the framing that Hird was a failed coach.

### Pillar 4 — A decade is a long time

He resigned in August 2015 **[historical record]**. That is ten years and nine months ago. In that decade he has been outside the senior coaching system entirely — no senior role, no public assistant position, no return-attempt that was rebuffed and re-tried **[historical record — verify against media coverage]**.

The AFL's formal sanctions against him expired in full **[historical record — verify against AFL determinations]**. His ASADA infraction notice was issued against the club's officials and the players' anti-doping cases proceeded through the Court of Arbitration for Sport in 2016 **[historical record]**. By 2026, the playing group involved has retired; the administrators of that period are gone from the club; the institutions that adjudicated the matter have moved through several reform cycles. A decade is not a token interval. It is, by any reasonable measure, a complete generation in football operations.

## The counterarguments — engaged, not dismissed

These are the real objections. They deserve serious treatment, not strawmen.

### Counter 1 — "The supplements saga disqualifies him."

This is the strongest objection and it cannot be waved away. The 2012 supplements program at Essendon resulted in 34 past and present players being issued infraction notices by ASADA, with the Court of Arbitration for Sport ultimately imposing backdated bans on 34 players in January 2016 **[historical record]**. Hird, as senior coach during the program, was suspended by the AFL for 12 months in August 2013 and resigned as coach in August 2015 **[historical record]**.

The objection has two forms. The strong form: he ran the football department in which the program occurred, regardless of the precise question of who designed the protocols, and that responsibility does not expire. The weak form: appointing him relitigates the story and creates a distraction.

**The honest response to the strong form:** he served the sanction the AFL imposed. He has not coached at AFL level since. If the proposition is that being the senior coach of a club where a sports-science program crossed a line is a permanent disqualification from ever coaching again, that is a defensible ethical position — but it is a *position*, not a logical necessity. The AFL itself has not taken that position; it would have built it into the original sanction if it intended to. The sanction it did build in was finite, and the finite period has long since expired.

**The honest response to the weak form:** the distraction argument is real but time-limited. The first month of an appointment would carry sustained media attention on the supplements era. By round eight of the season the story would be the football. The "distraction" framing is most often raised by people for whom the controversy is, in fact, *less* salient than they assume. The supporters who remember it most vividly are largely the Essendon supporters who would back the appointment most strongly.

### Counter 2 — "Player welfare questions during that period."

The most serious version of the supplements objection is not about doping per se. It is about whether players in the program had adequate informed consent, and what the duty of care of the senior coach was in that environment. The CAS finding on player culpability was that the players had failed in their personal anti-doping responsibility, but the broader question of how a duty-of-care environment is supposed to function in a senior football department was raised throughout the period **[historical record]**.

This objection has weight because the player-welfare question goes to the core of what a coach does. The response is not to deny that the question existed; the response is that the post-2015 evolution of player welfare governance at AFL clubs — independent medical reporting lines, club doctor reporting obligations, AFLPA representation in football-department decisions — has rebuilt the structural safeguards around any modern senior coach **[historical record — verify against AFLPA agreements]**. The same failure mode cannot occur in 2026 in the way it did in 2012, because the institutional architecture is different. Appointing Hird in 2026 is not appointing him into the 2012 environment.

### Counter 3 — "Ten years out of coaching is too long."

This is a football objection rather than an ethical one, and it is fair. The modern game has changed: the stoppage clusters, the high-zone defensive shape, the play-on-from-mark tempo template, the +6/-6 forward-defensive structures — these are post-Hird tactics. A coach who last set a senior gameplan in 2015 is, in 2026, a decade behind the tactical front.

**The response:** this is exactly the case for pairing him with a modern senior assistant. The Carlton entry in this news series argued that coaching success is now a department, not an individual. The same logic applies here. A senior coach whose football authority and identity-anchoring outstrips his current tactical literacy is matched with a senior assistant whose tactical currency outstrips his cultural authority. The combination — common in modern AFL appointments — is stronger than either alone. Sydney's Longmire-and-his-assistants model is one example; Geelong's Scott-and-Hinkley-era model in the late 2000s was another **[historical record]**.

This is the objection that *requires* the appointment to come with structural conditions: the assistant hires, the football-department resourcing, the modern analytics layer. Without those, the objection lands. With them, it is manageable.

### Counter 4 — "He has not asked to coach."

This is not a counterargument. It is a pre-condition. The Essendon board's first conversation is not with media commentators; it is with Hird himself, in private. If the answer is that he does not want it, the conversation ends and this piece is moot. If the answer is that he would consider it, every other counterargument is downstream of that.

## "Australia is the land of second chances" — making the framing rigorous

The framing risks being a slogan. It needs to do analytical work.

The Australian sporting record on second chances is not uniform. It is a documented pattern with a specific structure: figures who served their sanction in full, withdrew from the public sphere for a period proportionate to the seriousness of the offence, and returned through a credible institutional channel rather than a media campaign. The pattern matters because it distinguishes redemption from re-litigation.

**AFL examples that fit the pattern:**

- **Wayne Carey** returned to a media role after a period out of the game, and the AFL's relationship with him remains conditional rather than full — the example demonstrates that some returns are limited rather than complete **[historical record]**.
- **Gary Ablett Sr.** had off-field difficulties acknowledged after retirement and was reintegrated into the AFL Hall of Fame on football grounds in 2005 **[historical record — verify induction date]**.
- **Brendan Fevola** returned to the game and to media after well-documented personal struggles **[historical record]**.
- **Garry Lyon** returned to senior media work after a 2016 absence **[historical record]**.

The pattern across these cases is: the AFL system, the clubs, and the public have shown the capacity to assess returnees on the specifics of their conduct in the present rather than on the totality of past controversy. None of these are exact parallels to Hird's situation, because each controversy is its own structure. The point is not that any of these figures is comparable to Hird; the point is that the *mechanism of return* is well-precedented in Australian football, and the mechanism includes specific markers — sanction served, time elapsed, institutional reform around the original failure, and a credible vehicle for return.

**Hird meets the structural markers:**

- Sanction served (12 months, AFL-imposed, completed in 2014) **[historical record]**
- Time elapsed (over a decade)
- Institutional reform (the AFL anti-doping and football-department welfare frameworks have been rebuilt in the post-2016 period) **[historical record]**
- Credible institutional vehicle (an Essendon Football Club board appointment, made formally rather than through a media campaign)

The "land of second chances" framing is not a sentimental claim. It is an observation about the structural mechanism by which Australian sporting institutions, including the AFL, return figures to the game when the markers are met. Hird's case is, on the evidence, a case where the markers are met. Whether the Essendon board chooses to act on that is a separate question; the analytical claim is only that the structural argument for considering the appointment is sound.

## The verdict

James Hird should be on Essendon's shortlist for its next senior coach. Not because he is the obvious choice, and not because the past does not matter, but because the football case is real, the time elapsed is long, and the alternative — declining to interview him on the basis of a controversy whose institutional resolution is over a decade old — would be a process failure on the part of the club. The board's job is to assess every credible candidate. Hird, in 2026, is credible.

The argument is not that he must be appointed. The argument is that he must be considered, on the football merit, by a board that has read the data on his playing career (one of the great Essendon careers, 253 games, two flags, a Brownlow, 125 votes, finals weight that did not shrink **[data]**), the data on his coaching tenure (a 36W-30L-1D record across 2011-13 with an improving trajectory, in a football operation whose off-field failure was adjudicated externally **[data]**), and the structural markers of an AFL return. If the football case clears the bar — and the data argues that it does — the public-controversy considerations remain real but no longer dispositive.

Australia is the land of second chances. Not as a slogan, but as an institutional fact about how this country's sporting culture handles its complicated figures. The question is not whether second chances exist. The question is whether the Essendon board treats Hird's candidacy with the seriousness the football case warrants, or whether it forecloses the conversation before it begins. The first is the harder process and the right one. The second is the easier one and a failure of the board's actual job.

The Bombers are 1-9. The list has senior pieces. The structural diagnosis points at an identity coach paired with a modern tactical staff. The most thoroughly examined football figure of the modern Essendon era is sitting outside the system, has served the sanction the institutions imposed, and has been out of the game long enough that the football reasons to consider him outweigh the public-relations reasons not to.

That is the case. It does not have to be persuasive to every reader. It has to be a case the board takes seriously.

**Limits of this analysis:**

- This piece is an opinion piece with a declared position. It is not a neutral assessment. A reader who weights the supplements era more heavily than this piece does is entitled to a different conclusion on the same data.
- The "coaching case" pillars rest on the assumption that an identity-coach plus modern-tactical-assistant pairing is a viable structure in 2026 AFL coaching. That is supported by historical examples but is not a guaranteed outcome.
- The coaching record cited (36W-30L-1D, 2011-13) is associational with Hird's tenure. It does not isolate the coach's contribution from the playing list, the assistant group, or any other factor. A fair counter-read is that the 2011-13 Essendon list would have produced a winning record under most competent senior coaches.
- The 1999 season (2 games, 30 disposals **[data]**) reflects an injury-interrupted year, which is part of the explanation for Hird's absence from the longevity-weighted top 100. Reading the career on a per-game-quality basis tells a different story than reading it on a total-accumulation basis. Both reads are honest; this piece relies primarily on the per-game read.
- No invented win-loss totals as coach, no invented tribunal outcomes, no specific ASADA findings claimed beyond the public record framework described.

**What this piece is not:**

- It is not a denial that the supplements era happened or that it had consequences for the players involved.
- It is not a claim that Hird was uninvolved in the football department of that period.
- It is not a recommendation from this repository to the Essendon Football Club. The "Carlton next coach" entry's house rule applies here too: appointment decisions are club decisions. This piece lays out the case; it does not make the call.

What it is: an argument that the case deserves to be made and heard.
