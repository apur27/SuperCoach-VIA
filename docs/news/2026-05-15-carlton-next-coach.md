# Who should be the next Carlton coach?

## The one-line version

Carlton does not need a new voice. It needs a defensive-systems builder who has never sold a press conference in his life, and the board discipline to hold him through a year that will look worse before it looks better.

---

## What the data actually shows

Let's start with the numbers before the narratives.

**The 2026 collapse is real — but it has historical precedent.** Carlton are 1-8 through the first ten rounds (1-7 from the matches file through R9, with a R10 Brisbane loss confirmed in player data). **[data]** Their scoring average has fallen from 92.6 points per game in 2024 to 79.3 in 2026 — a **14.4% drop in scoring output in 18 months with the same core personnel**. **[data]** Points against per game has blown out from 85.5 (2024) to 101.5 (2026 partial). **[data]** Both ends of the ground have deteriorated; the defensive collapse is the more alarming signal.

Carlton have been here before. The 2002-2007 stretch produced six consecutive losing seasons (3, 4, 10, 4, 3, 4 wins — average 4.7, and 3.6 across the five non-2004 years) **[data]**, and the 2014-2019 era had its own nadir in 2018 (2-20 under Brendon Bolton). **[data]** Carlton's 12.5% win rate across the first eight games of 2026 sits between 2018's 9.1% full-season floor and the 13-18% full seasons of 2002, 2005, 2006 and 2007 — not unprecedented Carlton territory, and recoveries from floors like this have happened.

**The Voss record in context.** Final record from the data: 50W-53L-1D across 104 games (2022 R1 through 2026 R10), 48.1% win rate, aggregate margin +291 points across the 103 games in the matches CSV (R10 2026 vs Brisbane Lions is in player data only — confirmed loss, margin unavailable). **[data]** Carlton's win rate jumped 18 points (from 36.4% in 2021, the pre-Voss season, to 54.5% in 2022) as the coaching fingerprint took hold. Carlton made finals in 2023 (a 15-10-1 season, 26 games, including a preliminary final) and 2024 (13-11-0, 24 games). **[data]** The story is not a flat four-year failure — it is a two-year rise, a bad 2025, and a 2026 that has been a structural breakdown. A 2022-24 coaching tenure that produced two finals campaigns should not be rewritten by a 2025-26 collapse.

## The list is not bare — the system around it failed

This is the point the public narrative keeps missing, and the data is unambiguous.

**Sam Walsh** is averaging **28.22 disposals, 12.89 kicks, and 5.33 tackles per game** in 2026 — a top-shelf inside-mid season. **[data]** The R11 prediction model ranks him 10th in the league at 27 predicted disposals (competition rank, ties share the higher position). **[data]** That is not a list with a broken best player.

**Patrick Cripps** is still elite at the stoppage level. His 2026 averages: **24.89 disposals, 7.00 clearances, and 14.67 contested possessions per game** — both clearances and CP are the highest on the team. **[data]** He is not finished. He is being asked to do the inside work without the same outside support that once turned those clearances into scores.

**Jagga Smith** (22.78 disposals/game, age 20 in 2026) is the next-tier midfielder Carlton has been developing, and he is already the third-most prolific ball-winner on the list. **[data]** The inside-mid trio exists; the problem is the conversion layer around it.

**The list is thin behind the top three.** Carlton has two players in the model's top-30 predicted ball-winners for Round 11 (Walsh at #10, Smith Jagga at #22) and four in the top 50 (Florent Oliver and Newman Nic, both tied at #43). Contender clubs carry three to five in the top 30 alone — GWS has five and the Western Bulldogs have four. There is then a substantial rank drop from Carlton's seventh (Hewett George, rank 68 league-wide, only five games played this year) to its eighth and ninth (Haynes Nick and Williams Zac, both tied at rank 103). **[data]** Below that the next Carlton midfielder is Hollands Oliver at rank 116. The shape: an elite top three, two more credible mid-rotation pieces, then a notable thinning behind them.

**The forward end is the most underweighted weakness.** Mitch McGovern leads the club with 19 goals from 9 games (2.11 average **[data]** when counted on his 9 listed appearances; 2.38 if averaged only across games where he kicked **[data]**) but on only 10.56 disposals per game — he is a small-forward role player, not a high-disposal match-winner. Harry McKay is producing (11 goals from 8 games, 1.38 per game **[data]**) but not at number-one-forward rates. The scoring collapse is partly a structural failure around the midfield's clearance output and partly a forward line that is not converting what the midfield wins.

## The structural diagnosis: what type of coach Carlton actually needs

Run Carlton's failure through the lens council and three frames do almost all of the load-bearing work.

**The Structuralist lens carries the most weight.** Carlton's problem is that its shape does not travel. A team that wins clearances (Cripps leads the league's top mid-range in clearances per game) and then turns the ball over at the kick into 50 is a team without a connecting structure — without a defined forward-50 entry pattern, without a second-tall configuration that compounds week to week, without a stoppage shape that gets the ball to the same handball receiver three weeks in a row. The scoring collapse from 92.6 to 79.3 points per game is a coaching artefact. The next coach's first job is to install that connecting structure and defend it against the temptation to change it when results are slow.

**The Culture Custodian sits beside the Structuralist.** The contested standard that defined Carlton's 2022-24 identity was a tactic that came and went rather than an identity that held. Carlton's leaders — Cripps, Walsh, Hewett, the McKay brothers — are not the problem. The system around them is. A coach who installs a non-negotiable standard and then holds it through the mid-table years is what turns that leadership group into a senior finals core.

**The List Strategist is the third primary lens, and the one most easily underweighted in a rushed search.** The list arc is specific: past the bottom, not yet at the top of its window. Cripps and Hewett are deep into their primes. Walsh is at his peak. The ruck-forward slot is a live problem — Tom De Koning, who had anchored that role since 2022, moved to St Kilda ahead of 2026, leaving Marc Pittonet as current ruck cover. **[data]** That profile does not say "rebuild from scratch" and it does not say "contend next year." It says: we are inside a three-year window where the right structure, installed now, defended through one mid-table year, turns into finals football in 2028. The coach who fits is one who has internalised that timeline.

**Codex's structural read aligns with the data.** Its external assessment: Carlton need *"a coach who can make them less predictable, less clearance-dependent, and less fragile after half-time."* ABC analysis quoted by Codex had Carlton bottom two for moving the ball from their defensive half to an attacking position — **consistent with the scoring collapse the repo data shows**. The clearance engine (Cripps/Walsh/Hewett) is intact; what is broken is what happens after the clearance.

## The candidate profile — and who not to hire

The profile that fits the diagnosis is specific enough to be useful, and uncomfortable enough to be ignored.

**What to look for:** mid-forties to early-fifties, old enough to have run a unit at senior level for five-plus years, young enough that this is a decade-long proposition for them rather than a victory-lap appointment. A senior assistant at a club with a stable defensive identity — the defensive-coordinator-to-senior-coach pipeline has produced more durable appointments in the past fifteen years than any other route into the role. The coach whose public profile is indifferent ("not a media coach") and whose football-department profile is detailed. Comfortable telling a Brownlow medallist his second efforts on the inside have been unacceptable for three weeks.

**Codex's specific candidates** (noting these are external commentary, not verified against our data):
- **John Longmire** — structurally sound, hard to score against, authority to reset standards. Viable if Carlton want the safest senior appointment. The limitation is timeline: at 55, he is more a stabiliser than a decade-long coach, and Carlton may now need a medium-to-long build.
- **Hayden Skipworth** — if Carlton genuinely means "new direction," flagged as highly regarded for tactical nous with a deliberately broad apprenticeship across lines and programs. Fits a club that needs a modern strategist more than a headline name.
- **Jaymie Graham** — development plus method, long apprenticeship, strong player-development credentials. With Carlton returning to draft mode, a coach who can grow a next wave while sharpening structure may outperform a short-term big-personality hire.

**Codex explicitly avoids:** Nathan Buckley (brings standards and analysis but Carlton may need a lower-drama reset), Adam Simpson (continuity disguised as change), Ken Hinkley (himself reportedly acknowledging Carlton's list points to a longer build, which weakens the case for a 59-year-old appointment).

**FootyStrategy's structural ranking (Tier A/B/C):**

| Tier | Profile | Verdict |
|------|---------|---------|
| **A — target zone** | Senior assistant from a stable-defensive-identity club, mid-40s–50s, five-plus years running a unit, indifferent public profile | This is where the appointment should come from; typically the second or third name in any media list |
| **B — viable with caveats** | Proven senior coach from another club; system needs to travel to Carlton's list | Interview must be structural, not ceremonial — "describe three decisions you'd make differently for our list" |
| **C — high-risk** | High-profile ex-player without assistant track record, cultural-reset figure, or interstate appointment without a Victorian-club deputy | The case for each exists; the case against is that none of these levers address the structural problem |

## The three mistakes Carlton must not make

**Mistake one: the reactive high-profile hire.** The board reaches for the biggest available name to "signal seriousness." This answers the question "what coach fits the next press conference?" rather than "what coach fits the next three years?" Carlton has been here before. The cycle is well-documented.

**Mistake two: the cultural-reset appointment on a list that does not need a cultural reset.** The board, watching too many losses, concludes the problem is the room rather than the structure. They hire a charismatic figure with a reputation for "bringing standards back." Carlton's leaders are not the problem. The system around them is. A coach who walks in believing he is fixing the room rather than the shape will spend his first year resetting things that were not broken.

**Mistake three: the in-house promotion to preserve continuity when continuity is exactly what needs to break.** An in-house promotion can work — Melbourne's eventual 2021 flag was built on exactly that pattern — but only when the assistant being elevated is the architect of a distinct structural identity, not the inheritor of an indistinct one. The question to ask of any internal candidate: "which of the previous regime's structures would you keep and which would you change?" The answer needs to be specific.

**The lens that gets underweighted every time: the List Strategist.** Coaching searches default to talking about the coach in isolation. The right question is always: what coach fits this list, on this timeline, with these contracts coming up in the next two windows? Without the list arc as the frame, the search collapses into a personality contest, and the personality the room agrees on is almost never the coach the structure needs.

## The call — and the tripwire

**The appointment should come from Tier A.** The football department's job is to defend that ordering against the public-pressure pull toward Tier C, which will be loud.

**Codex's probability estimate: the next Carlton coach has approximately a 30% chance of lasting five or more years.** The framing: Carlton combines three coach-killing conditions — huge external expectation, internal leadership churn, and a list good enough to tempt shortcuts but not complete enough to justify them. If the board explicitly gives the new coach a three-year brief tied to draft integration, game-style evolution, and list balance, the probability rises toward 40%. If they judge him against 2027 ladder position alone, it drops to 20%. The base case is 30% because Carlton's history says patience is promised more often than it is practiced.

**The forward-50 tripwire (FootyStrategy):** a forward-50 setup that has visibly changed more than once in the first ten games while win-loss results remain flat or worse. That signature — structural restlessness paired with results that do not respond — is the unmistakable sign that the new coach is reacting to noise rather than installing a structure. Almost every other early-tenure pattern is recoverable. A coach who has not committed to a forward-50 shape by Round 10 of his first season is a coach who will not commit to one by Round 10 of his fifth.

**Secondary tripwire (FootyStrategy):** public framing that emphasises individual responsibility ("certain players have to be better") rather than structural responsibility ("our shape from stoppage was not where we wanted it") in three or more post-match conferences across the first ten games. That language pattern is the surface tell of a coach who is locating the problem outside the coaching craft — which is the failure mode the previous era ultimately collapsed into.
