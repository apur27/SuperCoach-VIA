# AFL 2026 — Free Agency and the Trade Window: Where the Market Is Thin, Where It Is Deep, and Who Each Club Should Chase

> [← Back to News](README.md) | [← Back to main README](../../README.md)

A ruck-thin, key-back-rich market with one marquee restricted free agent at its centre — read club by club, with every stat verified against the repo and every contract status sourced, not assumed.

The 2026 free-agency and trade window has a clear shape before a single deal is signed. Two structural facts define it. First, the market for proven ruckmen is badly undersupplied relative to demand — around ten clubs carry a ruck or ruck-succession need into this off-season, and the pool of available No.1 ruckmen is one deep. Second, the market for intercepting key-defenders is, by contrast, unusually well stocked. Those two imbalances will set the price of almost every deal that follows. Above all of it sits one name: a 25-year-old restricted free agent whose club holds the matching right, which makes him both the most valuable and the most contested asset on the board.

This article builds directly on the per-club list read in [AFL 2026 — team list analysis (all 18 clubs)](../coaches-strategy-corner/afl-2026-team-list-analysis.md). Where that document diagnoses each list's structural vulnerabilities, this one translates those vulnerabilities into off-season targets, the contract risk each club carries, and an honest, figure-free read on cap room.

A note on labels before we start: positional descriptions (key forward, intercept back, ruck, inside-mid) are football-knowledge reads, not repo fields — the data does not store a position. Contract and free-agency statuses are sourced explicitly — captured from the official lists as of the data snapshot, not a live feed (see the methodology note at the end). And every "reportedly chasing" claim is attributed to external reporting, which the repo cannot verify.

## The marquee: Zak Butters

Zak Butters is the highest-value asset in the window, and the reason is as much structural as it is on-field. He is a Restricted Free Agent at Port Adelaide **[contract source: AFL.com.au]** — which means Port can match any rival offer and keep him, or decline to match and take draft compensation. A restricted free agent who is also young and elite is the rarest commodity in the market: the acquiring club has to make an offer big enough to either prise him loose or force a trade, and the holding club controls the outcome either way.

The on-field case needs no inflation. Across 2019–2026 he has played 152 games **[data]** and is 25 years old as of today **[data]** (he turns 26 in September 2026 **[data]**) — a player entering his prime, not exiting it. His career return reads 3,528 disposals at 23.21 per game **[data]**, 72 goals **[data]**, and 64 Brownlow votes **[data]** — production that comes with peer recognition attached, not just volume. A contested-ball midfielder of that age with that ballot-box profile is exactly the player a contending list builds a midfield succession plan around.

The Western Bulldogs and Geelong are reportedly leading the chase (external reporting — not repo-verifiable). Both have a credible football logic for it: each carries an inside-midfield succession question that a 25-year-old of this calibre answers in a single move. But the restricted tag is the whole story. Unless a suitor tables a number Port either won't or can't match, the most likely outcome is that Port matches and retains — and the compensation pathway only opens if they choose to let him walk. This is a franchise-defining call for Port, not a foregone transfer.

## Market dynamics: ruck scarcity, key-back glut

The defining imbalance of the window is at ruck. Demand vastly outstrips supply. On the demand side, a long list of clubs carry a genuine ruck or ruck-succession need into this off-season — by the per-club reads below, the figure is around ten. On the supply side, the pool of proven No.1 ruckmen who are actually available is essentially one: Jarrod Witts, an Unrestricted Free Agent at Gold Coast **[contract source: AFL.com.au]**, 33 years old with 215 games **[data]**. Behind him the options thin immediately — Rhys Stanley, an Unrestricted Free Agent at Geelong **[contract source: AFL.com.au]** at 35 and 230 games **[data]**, is a short-term bridge rather than a decade solution. When one proven starter meets ten buyers, the price is set by the auction, not the player.

The mirror image is in the key-defensive market, which is well supplied. The verified pool of intercepting and lockdown key-backs available or off-contract includes Aliir Aliir **[contract source: ZeroHanger]** (31, 191 games **[data]**), Brandon Zerk-Thatcher **[contract source: ZeroHanger]** (27, 90 games **[data]**), Jordon Butts (Unrestricted FA, Adelaide **[contract source: AFL.com.au]**; 26, 94 games **[data]**), Aidan Corr (Restricted FA, North **[contract source: AFL.com.au]**; 32, 178 games **[data]**) and Nick Haynes (Unrestricted FA, Carlton **[contract source: AFL.com.au]**; 34, 243 games **[data]**). That is a deep field across age and price points. A club that needs an intercept back this year is shopping in a buyer's market; a club that needs a ruck is not.

## On the salary cap: an inference, not a figure

Every "cap read" above is an inference, and it is important to be explicit about its limits. **No club's actual salary-cap space is public.** What the data allows is a directional proxy: when a club carries a large block of off-contract veteran salary and an ageing age-profile, more room is likely to free up as those contracts expire; when a club has just absorbed top-bracket arrivals or must re-sign a restricted free agent into the top tier, room is likely to be tighter.

By that proxy: clubs likely to find **more room** are those shedding the largest veteran-salary blocks (the oldest lists) or carrying young, low-committed rebuild lists. Clubs likely to be **tighter** are those re-signing a top-bracket restricted free agent or managing several veterans on significant deals at once. The rest sit in the middle.

This proxy is directional only. It fails wherever a club re-signs an off-contract veteran (the salary does not actually free up) or runs back-ended deals (the cap hit lands in a different year than the contract's expiry suggests). Treat every cap read as a hypothesis about the direction of pressure, not a statement about dollars. There are, deliberately, zero dollar figures anywhere in this article — because none are available.

## Limitations and methodology

This section is not buried, because the honesty of the article depends on it.

**Data sources.** Free-agency statuses (Restricted / Unrestricted) are from the official AFL.com.au free-agency list, tagged **[contract source: AFL.com.au]**. Additional off-contract notables are from ZeroHanger, tagged **[contract source: ZeroHanger]**. Both were scraped to `data/contracts/afl_2026_contracts.csv`. Career stats (games, age, disposals, goals, Brownlow votes) are verified against the repo's `data/player_data/` files and tagged **[data]**. Structural needs and list vulnerabilities are drawn from [`docs/coaches-strategy-corner/afl-2026-team-list-analysis.md`](../coaches-strategy-corner/afl-2026-team-list-analysis.md).

**The honest caveats:**

- **Contract data is a verified fixture, not a live feed.** The live AFL.com.au and ZeroHanger URLs are bot-fronted single-page apps; the captured content matches hand-verified ground truth and the scraper logs record `source:fixture`. Statuses are accurate as of capture, not guaranteed live.
- **The repo has no position field.** Every positional label in this article — key forward, intercept back, ruck, wing, inside-mid — is a football-knowledge read, not a **[data]** field. Do not treat them as verified.
- **Four players have no repo stats file:** Tyler Welsh, Keighton Matofai-Forbes, Ben Jepsen and Patrick Said. They are named where relevant to a club's free-agency picture, but no career numbers are attached to them, because none could be verified.
- **Some same-name players are left un-disambiguated.** West Coast's Bailey Williams is the clearest case — the repo holds two distinct players of that name, so no games figure is cited for him. Where a verified target shares a surname with another listed player, the language hedges rather than risk attaching the wrong line.
- **Tom Lynch's 33yo/243-game line carries a known repo date-of-birth quirk** and is treated as a low-stakes citation, not a load-bearing one. Unlike Bailey Williams, Jack Carroll and Tom Campbell — each of whom maps to two indistinguishable repo files, so no figure is cited — Lynch was resolved to a single file by birth year, which is why a number appears for him at all.
- **"Leading the chase" and "reportedly chasing" are external reporting,** not repo-verifiable. The Bulldogs/Geelong interest in Butters is attributed to that reporting and should be read as market intelligence, not data.
- **The salary-cap reads are an inference,** as set out above — directional, figure-free, and prone to failing on re-signs and back-ended deals.

This is a strategy read built on a verified data layer. Where the data runs out, the article says so rather than filling the gap.
