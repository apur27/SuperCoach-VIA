# AFL 2026 — List Quality and Draft Pipeline: Where Every Club Stands

> [← Back to News](README.md) | [← Back to main README](../../README.md)

Every AFL club is in one of four states: premiership window open, window closing, rebuilding, or rebuilding faster than the ladder suggests. The simplest way to diagnose which state a club is in is to look at two things simultaneously — the quality of the list they have now, and the efficiency with which they have been building it through the National Draft over the past two decades. A club with elite list quality and a proven draft pipeline is a dynasty in progress. A club with a strong list built on low draft efficiency is a one-cycle risk. A club with high draft efficiency but not yet peak-list quality is an ascending threat. And a club with low draft efficiency and a thin list is one that needs to change how it builds, not just what it builds.

This article combines two lenses applied across all 18 clubs: the 2026 mid-season list assessment (list identity, cornerstone players, structural vulnerabilities, and cycle-stage) with the data-verified National Draft efficiency record from 2004 to 2025 — 1,529 tracked picks, graded by DraftGuru.com.au, across 22 draft seasons.

---

## The data framework

**Elite conversion rate** is the share of a club's National Draft picks from 2004–2025 that went on to an A+ or A career grade. League average is **10.1%** — meaning one-in-ten picks from the National Draft becomes a genuinely elite AFL player. **[data]**

The spread from top to bottom is nearly 3-to-1: the Western Bulldogs convert at 15.6%; St Kilda converts at 5.7%. **[data]** That gap is not noise. It reflects development infrastructure, player retention, and cultural stability across two decades.

### Sydney Swans

Sydney sit first in disposals **[data]** (396.5/g), first in goals **[data]** (17.5/g), first in tackles **[data]** (63.6/g), first in inside-50s **[data]** (64.4/g), and first in contested possessions in 2026. The list has no structural weakness at a level that matches how comprehensively it dominates the metrics. Charlie Curnow, acquired from Carlton in late 2025, adds a Coleman-calibre marking forward to an already elite midfield of Heeney, Warner, and Mills. The half-back unit of Blakey, Lloyd, and Mills-as-flex is the league benchmark in ball movement from defence. The one structural soft spot is ruck depth behind Grundy, and that is well-understood.

**[data]** Draft efficiency: 11.7% elite from 77 tracked picks (2 A+, 7 A), average pick 36.4, average career 68.6 games per pick — 6th in the competition. The 11.7% rate significantly understates the quality because Sydney's most productive picks are among the deepest in the dataset: Warner at pick 39 **[data]** (122 games, A), Parker at pick 40 **[data]** (328 games, A), Aliir at pick 44 **[data]** (191 games, A), Dawson at pick 56 **[data]** (166 games, A), Hannebery at pick 30 **[data]** (226 games, A+), Gulden at pick 32 **[data]** (105 games, A). Six of Sydney's nine elite National Draft players since 2004 came from picks after 25. The Bloods identify and develop overlooked talent from the back of the draft at a rate no other club matches at those pick depths.

The academy pipeline is the structural advantage that extends the window past draft position. Sydney pick late in the 20s range now; they compensate with the NSW academy, homegrown retention, and one surgical import per cycle (Curnow). The list philosophy ranking of first in the competition is earned. Realistic 2026 finish from the team list analysis: **1st–3rd, premiership favourite**.

### Brisbane Lions

Brisbane are built for marks and clearances — 1st for kicks **[data]** (252.5/g), 1st for marks **[data]** (122.8/g), 1st for clearances **[data]** (41.0/g) in 2026. The cornerstone tier is Neale, Andrews, McCluggage, Ashcroft, Dunkley, and Cameron — six players who each define a structural dimension of the side. Oscar Allen's arrival from West Coast in late 2025 added the third marking tall that was the only position the list had not settled. The vulnerability is the ageing midfield core: Neale, Lyons, and Dunkley are on the back nine, and Will Ashcroft's ability to step into a 30-disposal contested role defines the window length.

**[data]** Draft efficiency: 6.0% elite from 100 tracked picks — second lowest in the competition (1 A+, 5 A), average pick 36.4, average career 69.9 games per pick. The low efficiency rate requires context but not excuse. Brisbane have taken the most National Draft picks of any club across this window (100) because of compensation picks and their expansion-era access. But the rate is still the second worst in the competition even normalised for volume. Two of their six elite picks did not deliver that value at Brisbane: Elliot Yeo (2011 pick 30, 224 games A) **[data]** played his elite career at West Coast after departing as a rookie pick; Sam Docherty (2011 pick 12, 184 games A) **[data]** delivered his career at Carlton. The honest draft efficiency picture is: Brisbane drafted well enough to find Yeo and Docherty, then lost both.

The premiership core was assembled via the trade table, not the National Draft: Neale from Fremantle, Cameron from Adelaide, Dunkley from the Bulldogs. That is not a criticism — it is the most defensible list-building model at the competent end of the trade cycle. Brisbane draft functionally, import elite, and retain. The model is proven by three consecutive grand final appearances.

### Sydney note on Dawson

One data integrity point worth flagging: **Jordan Dawson** is credited as a Sydney pick (2015 pick 56, 166 games, A) **[data]** in the efficiency table — he was drafted by Sydney but delivered most of his elite career at Adelaide after a 2021 trade. This is consistent with the caveat above: the table credits the drafting club, not the retaining club. Adelaide's 12.5% efficiency rate does not credit them for Dawson; Sydney's 11.7% does include him. The underlying talent identification is Sydney's; the career games are Adelaide's.

### GWS Giants

GWS are 2nd in disposals **[data]** (395.8/g), 1st in rebound-50s **[data]** (42.4/g), and 1st in uncontested possessions in 2026. The list is possession-heavy and half-back dominant, built around a midfield that is now reinforced by Clayton Oliver from Melbourne — one of the largest single-player acquisitions in recent trade history. The existing core of Greene (pick 11, 274 games, A+) **[data]**, Whitfield (pick 1, 269 games, A) **[data]**, Callaghan (pick 3, 84 games, A) **[data]**, Taylor, and Green makes GWS the deepest inside-mid and half-back structure in the competition. The structural soft spot is ruck — Briggs is functional, not dominant.

**[data]** Draft efficiency: 13.8% elite from 80 picks — 2nd highest in the competition (1 A+, 10 A), average pick 25.6 (2nd lowest, reflecting expansion-era top picks), average career 84.6 games per pick — the highest in the competition. The 84.6 games average is the most important single number in the entire efficiency table: GWS did not just find elite players, they developed every pick to play more games on average than any other club. This is an infrastructure story as much as a talent-identification one. The development system at GWS produced average careers of 84.6 games from 80 picks, meaning even the non-elite picks played meaningful AFL football. No other club comes close.

The efficiency data carries a retention asterisk: Tim Taranto (2016 pick 2, 184 games, A) **[data]** moved to Richmond; Taylor Adams (2011 pick 13, 229 games, A) **[data]** moved to Collingwood; Jack Steele (2014 pick 24, 216 games, A) **[data]** moved via St Kilda to Melbourne. The salary cap has forced exits that a less constrained expansion club would not have made. The model develops well and exports well — sometimes involuntarily.

### Fremantle

Fremantle are 2nd in contested possessions **[data]** (137.6/g), 3rd in tackles **[data]** (62.1/g), and 2nd in hit-outs **[data]** (45.4/g). The list has the best twin-ruck setup in the competition (Jackson and Darcy), a contested-ball midfield anchored by Serong and Brayshaw, and a rebuilt half-back unit. The one structural gap that has persisted across multiple windows is a settled contested-marking key forward: the side dominates the ball but the conversion rate lags because there is no tall to hold up inside-50 entries. Shai Bolton's arrival from Richmond added the small-forward pressure piece; the forward tall remains unsettled.

**[data]** Draft efficiency: 9.0% elite from 89 picks (3 A+, 5 A), average pick 39.3, average career 68.1 games per pick — just below league average. The 9.0% headline understates Fremantle's talent identification capability at the back of the draft. Lachie Neale at pick 58 **[data]** (308 games, A+) is the highest-impact late-round find in this entire dataset. Luke Ryan at pick 66 **[data]** (191 games, A) is a second A-grade from outside the top 50. Three of Fremantle's eight elite picks came after pick 50. The talent-identification machinery operates at every level of the draft, not just the top end. The retention loss that most undermines the headline: Neale moved to Brisbane in 2018. The club that found him at pick 58 did not retain him through his prime.

The 2026 list is built for the next three seasons: Serong (2019 pick 8, 137 games, A+) **[data]** and Brayshaw (2017 pick 2, 183 games, A) **[data]** are both inside 26 and signed long-term. Murphy Reid's arrival via recent draft adds the next-generation creator. The trade-table answer to the forward-tall question is the single acquisition that separates this list from premiership favourite.

### Collingwood

Collingwood sit 4th for disposals **[data]** (388.4/g) and 2nd for marks **[data]** (111.4/g) in 2026, built on a possession-and-control identity that has Nick Daicos averaging 36.6 disposals per game **[data]** — first in the competition. The premiership core from 2023 is intact: Daicos (pick 4, 107 games, A+) **[data]**, Moore, De Goey (pick 5, 194 games, A) **[data]**, Sidebottom (pick 11, 365 games, A) **[data]**, Pendlebury (pick 5, 435 games, A+) **[data]**. Dan Houston's arrival from Port Adelaide adds the rebound half-back the list lacked. The chronic structural weakness is ruck — 15th for hit-outs in 2026 **[data]**, with no dominant tap since Grundy was traded to Sydney — and the ageing curve on Pendlebury and Sidebottom makes generational succession the next three-season question.

**[data]** Draft efficiency: 12.9% elite from 85 picks — 3rd highest in the competition (2 A+, 9 A), average pick 40.1, average career 64.8 games per pick. Average pick of 40.1 is the most striking number: Collingwood are the highest-efficiency Victorian club in this dataset despite using the deepest average pick position of any above-average club. Josh Daicos at pick 57 **[data]** (164 games, A), Maynard at pick 30 **[data]** (242 games, A), Beams at pick 29 **[data]** (177 games, A), Grundy at pick 18 **[data]** (255 games, A) — four A-grade players from picks 18 to 57. The Magpies do not need top-five picks to find elite players. This is the data-confirmed version of what the competition has suspected for years.

The tension in the 2026 picture is clear: a 12.9% historical efficiency rate and a 40.1 average pick means the club has been extraordinarily efficient with relatively modest draft assets — but the ageing curve now requires those picks to replace Pendlebury-and-Sidebottom tier players. No draft produces two A+ players per cycle on average.

### Geelong

Geelong sit 9th for disposals **[data]**, 5th for contested possessions **[data]**, 5th for inside-50s **[data]** and 2nd for tackles **[data]** (62.5/g) in 2026. The list operates on the "soft-rebuild-while-contending" model that has delivered four premierships since 2007: average drafting + above-average late extraction + elite trade-table imports + player retention. Dangerfield was traded from Adelaide. Cameron was traded from GWS. Smith was traded from the Bulldogs. Worpel was traded from Hawthorn in 2025. The homegrown core (Selwood legacy retired, Hawkins retired, Stewart pick 40, Guthrie, Holmes) provides the foundation; the trade table provides the premium.

**[data]** Draft efficiency: 10.1% elite from 89 picks — exactly at league average (3 A+, 6 A), average pick 42.0 — highest average pick of any above-average or at-average club in the competition, average career 66.9 games per pick. Three of nine elite Geelong picks from the National Draft came after pick 40: Hawkins (pick 41, 359 games, A+) **[data]**, Stewart (pick 40, 204 games, A+) **[data]**, Egan (pick 62, 59 games, A) **[data]**. Geelong convert at exactly league average despite drafting with the deepest average pick in their efficiency tier. The late-extraction performance is the structural advantage that offsets the pick-depth penalty.

The data does not and cannot account for Dangerfield's 370 games at Geelong after leaving Adelaide **[data]**, or Cameron's 200+ games after leaving GWS **[historical record — career games split not verified in data]**, or Smith's arrival from the Bulldogs. Those players are credited to the clubs that drafted them — Adelaide, GWS, and the Bulldogs respectively — in the efficiency table. Geelong's actual on-field elite-player count is materially higher than 10.1% suggests because the club imports elite players the draft credits elsewhere.

### Gold Coast Suns

Gold Coast have been transformed in 18 months. Christian Petracca from Melbourne, Jamarra Ugle-Hagan from the Bulldogs, and Daniel Rioli from Richmond have been added to a core of Rowell (pick 1, 118 games, A+) **[data]**, Anderson (pick 2, 141 games, A) **[data]**, King, and Miller. The side now sits 2nd for inside-50s **[data]** (58.9/g) and 4th for hit-outs **[data]**, with three genuine marking-tall options inside 50 where prior years had one. The 2026 question is cohesion: three new Tier 1 players in one window is an 18–24 month integration test.

**[data]** Draft efficiency: 8.2% elite from 61 picks — below league average (1 A+, 4 A), average pick 24.1 — lowest average pick in the competition, average career 75.6 games per pick. The expansion-club paradox is visible in these numbers: Gold Coast received priority picks in their early years and used the earliest average picks (24.1) of any club, yet converted at below-average rates. Dion Prestia (pick 9, 246 games, A) **[data]** and Thomas Lynch (pick 11, 243 games, A) **[data]** both left — Prestia to Richmond, Lynch to West Coast — taking two A-grade careers to clubs that drafted less effectively. The 2019 correction — holding both picks 1 and 2 and converting them to A and A+ — is the inflection point the numbers show. Rowell and Anderson were drafted, developed, and retained. That retention discipline had not been present in the 2010–2018 window.

The 61-pick sample is the smallest in the dataset — Gold Coast's short competition history thins the denominator. The 8.2% rate is the most likely to change as the 2020–2025 cohort matures. Whether Ben King, Elijah Hollands, and the incoming trade players grade A is the live question.

### Port Adelaide

One notable retention loss: Chad Wingard (pick 6, 218 games, A) **[data]** moved to Hawthorn.

The 2026 list is one big-game contested-ball win away from a premiership tilt; the structural ceiling is whether Horne-Francis can consistently deliver the centre-bounce dominance that was promised with pick 1 from North Melbourne.

## What the data says across the competition

**The 3-to-1 efficiency spread is not luck.** The Western Bulldogs at 15.6% elite conversion convert National Draft picks at nearly three times the rate of St Kilda at 5.7%. **[data]** Across 90 picks and 88 picks respectively, that spread is statistically robust. It reflects development infrastructure, cultural stability, and retention capability — three things that can be built deliberately but cannot be bought quickly.

**The highest-efficiency clubs find elite players everywhere in the draft, not just at the top.** The Bulldogs' best four picks in this dataset are Bontempelli (pick 4), Macrae (pick 6), Liberatore (pick 41), and Daniel (pick 46). **[data]** Sydney's six elite picks after pick 25 include an A+ at pick 30 and an A at pick 39, 40, 44, 56, and 32. **[data]** Fremantle found an A+ at pick 58. **[data]** West Coast found an A at pick 77. **[data]** The clubs at the top of the efficiency table are not simply buying results with top picks — they are finding results where other clubs see waste.

**The two trade models that layer on top of drafting are distinct.** Geelong (10.1% efficiency, average pick 42.0) **[data]** reaches league average through late extraction and then imports elite players via trade — Dangerfield, Cameron, Smith — to provide the premium tier the draft position cannot reliably deliver. GWS (13.8% efficiency, average pick 25.6) **[data]** converted expansion-era premium picks into the deepest development infrastructure in the competition, then used that infrastructure to earn trade table credibility. Both models produce flags; neither is accessible to mid-table clubs trying to compress a rebuild.

**Below-average efficiency clubs face a structural list ceiling that trade-table activity alone does not fix.** St Kilda at 5.7%, Brisbane at 6.0%, Richmond at 8.7%, Essendon at 8.7% **[data]** — all four clubs have been active at the trade table across this window. St Kilda imported Steele, De Koning, Macrae. Brisbane imported Neale, Cameron, Dunkley. Richmond imported Taranto and Hopper. Essendon imported McKay and various mid-tier additions. In each case, the trade-table activity addressed the immediate list gap without changing the underlying pipeline efficiency. The list ceilings these clubs face are not purely a function of draft-pick depth; they are a function of whether the picks used are being converted into elite players at and below the league average rate.

**The retention question sits outside this dataset but inside every list manager's job.** This analysis credits the drafting club — not the club that retained the player. Essendon drafted Houli; Richmond retained him. **[historical record]** Adelaide drafted Dangerfield; Geelong retained him. **[historical record]** Fremantle drafted Neale; Brisbane retained him. **[historical record]** North Melbourne drafted Horne-Francis; Port Adelaide retained him. **[historical record]** The gap between a club's efficiency rate (which credits the draft) and its on-field elite-player count (which credits the retention) is where the trade-table story lives. The clubs at the top of the efficiency table — Bulldogs, GWS, Collingwood, Adelaide, Melbourne — are not all the clubs with the highest on-field elite-player counts. The clubs that convert both (Sydney, Brisbane through trade-table retention, Geelong through a different mechanism) are the ones who win flags.

## Methodology and caveats

**Files read:** `data/drafts/draftguru_enrichment.csv` and `data/drafts/afl_draft_history.csv`, joined on `(year, pick)`, 2004–2025 National Draft only. Grades (A+/A/B+/B/C+/C) from DraftGuru.com.au as captured in `draftguru_enrichment.csv`. List quality assessment from `docs/coaches-strategy-corner/afl-2026-team-list-analysis.md`. 2026 team stat rankings from `docs/afl-team-analysis-2026.md` via the team list analysis cross-reference section (rounds 1–9 season-to-date).

**Join caveat:** The join on (year, pick) between the Wikipedia draft history and DraftGuru produced 1,529 matched rows from 1,733 Wikipedia rows and 1,538 DraftGuru rows. Unmatched rows reflect year-pick misalignments from priority picks, compensation picks, and cross-source naming differences. The 1,529 matched rows are the analysis population; picks outside this population are excluded. This is a lower bound on the total National Draft picks across the window.

**Efficiency credits the drafting club, not the retaining club.** All games totals reflect the player's full career across all clubs, not only the drafting club. A player drafted by Club A who played 250 games at Club B increases Club A's efficiency rate and average games per pick. This is a talent-identification measure, not a talent-retention measure.

**Club name standardisation:** Kangaroos → North Melbourne; West Coast Eagles → West Coast; Sydney Swans → Sydney; Greater Western Sydney → GWS Giants applied for join consistency.

**2026 stat rankings are season-to-date (rounds 1–9) and subject to change.** All tagged **[data]** stat anchors from the team list analysis reflect the state of play at mid-2026 and may differ from full-season rankings.

**No causal claims.** The association between draft efficiency and on-field success is observable in this data; it is not established as causal. Multiple confounding factors (list depth, coaching, trade-table activity, salary-cap management, injuries) affect on-field outcomes independently of draft conversion rates.
