## The Milera problem

Nasiah Wanganeen-Milera is the connective tissue of St Kilda's half-back line — and he is out for several rounds.

In 2026 he was averaging **26.75 disposals, 19.5 kicks, 4.38 rebound 50s, and 4.88 inside 50s per game** with 7 goals from 8 appearances. **[data]** That combination — elite kicking volume, both a rebound threat and an inside-50 source, secondary goal production from a half-back — is extraordinarily rare. His 2025 was even better (29.9 disposals per game), and the trajectory across four seasons shows a player who genuinely arrived as a top-10 talent. **[data]**

What Milera gave Ross Lyon was a player who could break lines from defence with the ball in hand, then show up inside 50 as a third forward option. Remove him and the St Kilda game plan becomes shorter, slower, more predictable. The kick from half-back that was splitting zones now has to come from a player who is either less accurate (Hill, Windhager) or less willing to take on the corridor (Wilkie, who is primarily an intercept player).

**FootyStrategy read:** Lyon typically asks his half-back line to drive the ball long through the corridor; Milera was the one executing the attacking version of that role. Without him, the Saints' half-back structure leans into the defensive shape — Wilkie anchors the intercept, Hill takes more of the kick-out chain — but the attacking dimension that made St Kilda's transition so efficient comes off. The replacement configuration is safer, not better.

Bradley Hill is the closest natural substitute: kick-heavy (15.78 kicks per 23.33 disposals), high-marking (7.22 per game), a wing/half-back hybrid who has played this kind of transition role at both Hawthorn and Fremantle before landing at St Kilda. His increased touch reflects the extra midfield load, not the structural fix in defence.

**The gap the model can't paper over:** no player currently predicted in the St Kilda 22 combines Milera's kicking volume *and* rebound-50s *and* inside-50s in the same line. The Saints' attack becomes more centrally-sourced and less left-field-dangerous without him.

## Richmond's 2026 reality — and their narrow path

The Tigers are **1 win from 9 starts**, averaging 64.9 points for and 107.7 points against, with an average losing margin of 42.8 points. **[data]** This is a list in transition, not a team that should be considered competitive on numbers alone.

Their sole win came in Round 9 in Perth against West Coast — the only team plausibly worse than them. The margins tell the story: -4, -68, -60, -42, -56, -75, -54, then +11, then -37. **[data]** This is not a team running a soft schedule badly; it is a team absorbing a developmental year and occasionally getting close.

The inside midfield is the one genuine competitive strength. **Taranto Tim** (23.14 disposals, 5.00 tackles/game), **Hopper Jacob** (22.12 disposals, contested possession machine) and **Ross Jack** (23.56 disposals, 5.22 tackles) are an inside trio that can win clearances against any opponent. **[data]** The breakdown has always been what happens after the clearance: the skill errors, the broken chains, the league-worst scoring output.

**Jayden Short** (25.25 disposals, 19.25 kicks, 8.38 marks/game) is Richmond's answer to the Milera problem — a kick-heavy half-back distributor who can break lines and find targets long. **[data]** The model has him at 23 for Round 11 (but note Richmond are the most over-predicted team in the backtest at +0.53 disposals per player — mentally shade that to 22). **[data]**

**FootyStrategy read:** Richmond's path in this game is not to win possession — St Kilda will likely edge that. Their path is to make the game contested, territorial, and slow. Win clearances through the inside trio. Kick long to Lynch and let him mark or draw free kicks. Keep the inside-50 count close at quarter time. Every score they put up against St Kilda's reorganised half-back (which is now Hill-in-Milera's-role + Wilkie) is a score earned from a structure that is weaker than it was eight days ago.

The closing-game problem is real. When Richmond got to half-time close in Rounds 2 (Carlton, -4 at some point) and 9 (West Coast, ultimately +11), they showed they can stay in it. Whether they can close is the open question this year.

## Three match-ups that decide it

**1. The Milera replacement vs Richmond's forward entry**

Whoever Lyon puts into Milera's role — Hill most likely — will face a harder test than they've had all year. Richmond won't have the polish to expose it systematically, but if Short can hit moving targets on the wing and Hopper's clearances find Long or Lynch inside 50, the Saints' reorganised half-back becomes the release valve for Richmond's attack. A bad quarter from Hill in this role = Richmond on the scoreboard.

**2. Clearance count**

Taranto, Hopper, and Ross against Flanders, Steele (if available), and Sinclair in the middle. Richmond will be competitive here — they regularly win clearances in games they lose badly, which tells you the conversion is the issue. If they win clearances *and* conversion lifts even slightly (Lynch getting one or two contested marks, Short finding an overlap), the game gets interesting.

**3. The wing battle — Richmond's most underrated asset**

Jayden Short at half-back has to find a wing target who can run the corridor against Hill or Windhager. This matchup is underrated because most analysis focuses on the two midfields. If Ross on the wing can get 3-4 kicks inside 50 off Short hits, that is the Richmond attacking structure working. If St Kilda's wing rotation smothers Short's kick, the Tigers go back to hoping for a contested goal.

## The call — and the tripwire

**FootyStrategy:** Richmond by 11.

Hear it out. St Kilda are the better team and should win. But they lose their best ball-user, enter this game having dropped R7 by 1 point to Adelaide (showing their own closing-game inconsistency), and face a Richmond midfield that is legitimately competitive at clearance level. The Codex external read (which had no access to our H2H data) was 35-40% Richmond — which is roughly right as a *range*. Our H2H and form data would put Richmond's true probability closer to 25-30%.

But probability is not prediction. A Richmond by 11 call reflects:
- Milera's absence weakens the St Kilda transition by a measurable amount
- Richmond's inside mid engine is their genuine strength and this is a game type that suits it (slow, contested)
- St Kilda's form (4W-4L, +12.4 avg margin) is good but not dominant; they can be beaten
- The 2025 R23 fixture (St Kilda by 4) shows this is a game that *can* be close

**Tripwire:** If St Kilda lead the inside-50 count at half-time — if the Saints are winning territory despite losing Milera's rebound — flip this call. An inside-50 differential in St Kilda's favour at the main break tells you Wilkie and Hill have absorbed the workload cleanly and Richmond's inside engine isn't converting into ground position. That game ends St Kilda by 20+.

Watch the inside-50 count at half-time. Everything else flows from it.
